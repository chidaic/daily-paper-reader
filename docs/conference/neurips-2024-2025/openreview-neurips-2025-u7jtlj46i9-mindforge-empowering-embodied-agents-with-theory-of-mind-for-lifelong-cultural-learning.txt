Abstract 

Embodied agents powered by large language models (LLMs), such as Voyager, promise open-ended competence in worlds such as Minecraft. However, when powered by open-weight LLMs they still falter on elementary tasks after domain-specific fine-tuning. We propose M IND FORGE , a generative-agent framework for cultural lifelong learning through explicit perspective taking. We introduce three key innovations: (1) a structured theory of mind representation linking percepts, beliefs, desires, and actions; (2) natural inter-agent communication; and (3) a multi-component memory system. Following the cultural learning framework, we test MIND FORGE in both instructive and collaborative settings within Minecraft. In an instructive setting with GPT-4, MIND FORGE agents powered by open-weight LLMs significantly outperform their Voyager counterparts in basic tasks yielding 

3× more tech-tree milestones and collecting 2.3× more unique items than the Voyager baseline. Furthermore, in fully collaborative settings, we find that the performance of two underachieving agents improves with more communication rounds, echoing the Condorcet Jury Theorem. MIND FORGE agents demonstrate sophisticated behaviors, including expert-novice knowledge transfer, collaborative problem solving, and adaptation to out-of-distribution tasks through accumulated cultural experiences. 

1 Introduction 

The development of generally capable agents marks a significant shift in advancing artificial in-telligence, transitioning from assimilating data to generating novel knowledge through embodied interactions with open-ended environments [ 1– 4 ]. Classical approaches leveraging reinforcement learning [ 5, 6] and imitation learning [ 7 ] often struggle with generalization and exploration, as agents tend to converge on repetitive behaviors in static environments [ 8]. To address these limitations, researchers have sought to emulate human-like lifelong learning capabilities, developing systems that can continuously acquire, update, and transfer knowledge over extended periods [ 9, 10 ].The advent of large language models (LLMs) has accelerated this pursuit, enabling the development of agents such as Voyager [ 11 ] that can apply internet-scale knowledge to continuously explore, plan, and acquire new skills in partially observable, open-ended environments such as Minecraft. So central is the social context to our existence that the Social Intelligence Hypothesis posits that our cognitive capabilities evolved primarily to navigate the complexities of social life [ 12 , 13 ]. This isolated learning becomes particularly problematic when coupled with these agents’ reliance on closed LLMs like GPT-4. Wang et al. [11] note that “VOYAGER requires the quantum leap in code generation quality from GPT-4 which GPT-3.5 and open-source LLMs cannot provide” . Our experiments (Section 5) corroborate this observation, demonstrating that replacing GPT-4 with open LLMs renders the Voyager agent incapable of even the most rudimentary tasks, such as collecting dirt in Minecraft. We ask: can an open LLM-powered agent, which struggles with basic tasks, improve through collaboration with others? To address this question, we introduce the MIND FORGE framework, inspired by cultural learning, which contends that individuals internalize skills and strategies by observing others’ perspectives, using Theory of Mind (ToM) to infer goals, beliefs, and intentions and thereby deepen social learning [ 14 –18 ]. Our framework reframes knowledge distillation [ 19 ] as a social, test-time process: agents refine their beliefs through multi-turn dialogue with expert peers, without gradient updates. This in-teractive distillation supports test-time compute scaling [20 ], enabling agents to improve performance via communication, memory retrieval and perspective-taking. By structuring communication across turns, MIND FORGE also contributes to multi-turn LLM agent research [ 21 , 22 ], showing that open models can match GPT-4 through collaboration alone. Moreover, our analysis of weak-weak settings highlights a compute-performance tradeoff at inference, linking collaboration to test-time compute considerations [20]. Figure 1a provides an overview of our agents in action. Concretely, MIND FORGE extends Voyager by incorporating three key improvements: (1) perspective-taking through a causal ToM template [ 23 ] linking agent percepts, beliefs, desires, and actions; (2) a natural language communication interface between agents; and (3) memory updates following the Soar cognitive architecture [ 24 ], including semantic memory of task and environment-related knowledge and episodic memory .Empirically, we instantiate MIND FORGE agents in Instructed Learning (mixed-expertise) and 

Collaborative Learning (matched-expertise) settings within the cultural learning framework [ 15 ]. MIND FORGE agents powered by open-weight LLMs outperform the original Voyager architecture [11 ] on two seemingly trivial tasks by more than 40% . We identify Voyager’s specific failure cases: (1) false beliefs and (2) code generation , and show how our method overcomes them through communication and perspective-taking. To assess lifelong learning, we run MIND FORGE on the Minecraft tech-tree and find that it surpasses open-weight Voyager variants in both key tech-tree milestones and the number of unique items obtained. Section 5.5 further shows that when both agents begin with the same limited capabilities, a single round of GPT-4 seeding followed by just seven turns of peer-to-peer dialogue lifts the population-level success rate from 62% to 79% , revealing a Condorcet-style “many-minds” boost that stems from richer interaction rather than larger models. This generalization in post-collaborative settings paves the way for agents that can continuously adapt through social interaction, reducing the reliance on large-scale computing resources for training and fine-tuning the underlying language models. 22 Related Work 

Theory of Mind in Embodied Agents Early attempts to equip machines with mental-state rea-soning drew on deep learning [ 25 – 27 ], reinforcement learning [ 28 ], and Bayesian models [ 29 ]. These systems were either confined to toy 2-D worlds [ 25 , 26 ] or failed to generalize to richer environments [ 27 ]. The emergence of large language models (LLMs) reignited interest in ToM, sparking evaluations of their putative capabilities [ 23 , 30 – 32 ]. Although evidence so far suggests that LLMs lack genuine ToM, their breadth of knowledge has inspired a new wave of prompt-based frameworks [ 33 – 36 ]: the first two focus on perspective-taking, while the latter pair ground reasoning in explicit causal models. BIP-ALM remains the sole example that integrates such a framework with an LLM [37, 38]. 

LLM-based Multi-Agent Systems LLMs have demonstrated overwhelming success in performing a variety of single-agent tasks ranging from question answering and text generation to code synthesis and complex reasoning in embodied settings [ 11 , 39 , 40 ]. This success has started a growing interest in developing LLM-based multi-agent systems [ 21 , 22 , 41 – 43 ], where language-based communication is a central component. For instance, CoELA [ 21 ] introduce a modular multi-agent framework that integrates memory, perception and execution, while COMBO [ 41 ] present a multi-agent cooperative framework, that deals with collaboration in partial egocentric views of the environment. However, these frameworks lack an explicit belief representation and Theory-of-Mind reasoning. Recently, benchmarks like MineCollab [ 44 ], designed for collaborative reasoning, find that current LLM agents are not suited for multi-agent collaboration in long-horizon embodied scenarios, a gap that MIND FORGE addresses by endowing agents with structured perspective taking capabilities. 

Single-agent Frameworks Recent research on long-horizon tasks in single-agent systems differs architecturally from MIND FORGE ’s unified collaborative learning framework. For example, Ex-peL [ 45 ] and CLIN [ 46 ] emphasize experiential learning through vectorized trajectories or causal abstractions but lack explicit belief graphs and integration across episodic, semantic, and procedural memories. Frameworks like DEPS [47] and ADaPT [48] enable in-episode self-correction via itera-tive refinement, yet insights are often ephemeral, confined to the context window without persistent storage or structured models of others’ beliefs. SSO [ 49 ] provides procedural memory through skill libraries but omits episodic/semantic layers and social reasoning for targeted teaching or belief revision. Similarly, Adam [ 50 ] structures world knowledge as causal graphs in open environments, while Optimus-1 [51] builds hierarchical semantic graphs from experiences. 

3 Background 

Voyager Voyager agents consist of three main components: (1) an automatic curriculum generator, (2) a skill library, and (3) an iterative prompting mechanism. The automatic curriculum proposes progressively complex tasks, driving continuous learning. The skill library stores and retrieves learned behaviors, facilitating knowledge accumulation and transfer. The iterative prompting mechanism generates and refines executable code based on environmental feedback, allowing for adaptive behavior. Voyager demonstrated significant capabilities in item discovery, technology progression, and skill generalization. This system established a new benchmark in open-world AI agents, showcasing the potential for lifelong learning and adaptation in complex, dynamic environments. The Voyager framework serves as a base for our lifelong collaborative learning agents within Minecraft. 

BigToM Causal Template The BigToM causal template [ 23 ], provides a structured framework for representing Theory of Mind (ToM) scenarios as causal graphs. This template comprises key components including context, desire, percept, belief, causal event, and resulting actions. By representing ToM scenarios in this causal structure, the template allows for systematic manipulation of variables such as an agent’s perceptions, beliefs, and actions in response to environmental changes. This approach enables the creation of diverse, well-controlled ToM scenarios, capturing the complex causal relationships between an agent’s mental states and their behavior in social situations. The causal graph representation facilitates a more nuanced and systematic exploration of ToM reasoning, allowing for the examination of various inference patterns such as forward belief, forward action, and backward belief inferences in social cognitive tasks. 3Comm. 4 Method 

To grant MIND FORGE agents the ability to learn through collaborative social interactions, we intro-duce the following components for communication and perspective-taking: (i) an explicit structured representation linking percepts, beliefs, desires, and actions (Section 4.1), (ii) perspective taking so that the agent can reason over self-beliefs and those about its collaborators (Section 4.2), (iii) a com-munication medium that the agents can use to share information in natural language (Section 4.3), and (iv) the ability to utilize different types of memory subsystems (Section 4.4). 

4.1 Structured Representation of Own Mental States: Beliefs, Desires, and Intentions 

For MIND FORGE agents to develop effective theory of mind capabilities, they must first possess structured representations of their own mental states. This explicit modeling of the self is crucial for recursive social reasoning : enabling agents not only to reason about their own beliefs, goals, perceptions, and how these might lead to their actions, but also to form a similar mental model for other agents. This mirrors findings in developmental psychology, where children first develop an awareness of their own thoughts, feelings, and goals before being able to infer the mental states of others [ 52 , 53 ]. This is further supported by neuroscientific research that has also identified distinct neural mechanisms for self-cognition and social cognition [54, 55]. We draw on the classical Belief-Desire-Intention (BDI) framework [ 56 ] and operationalize it us-ing the BigToM causal template (Section 3) [ 23 ]. In MIND FORGE , beliefs represent the agent’s current understanding of the world inferred from sensory perception, including interactions with other agents. Desires encode the agent’s goals, both generated by an automatic curriculum and those arising from collaborative interactions. Intentions correspond to concrete executable actions, implemented as generated code—and so, consistent with BigToM [ 23 ], we treat “intentions” and “actions” interchangeably in implementation. However, we note that this equivalence does not always hold conceptually: intentions refer to internal commitments or plans, while actions are observable behaviors that may diverge from intentions. For a deeper treatment of this distinction, see [57]. 

Belief Structure. We categorize a MIND FORGE agent’s beliefs into four key categories, illustrated in Figure 3: i. perception-related beliefs , formed from direct sensory input via the Minecraft API; ii. 

task-related beliefs , inferred from the agent’s reflection on its upcoming objectives; iii. interaction-related beliefs , derived from chat messages during task execution; and iv. partner-related beliefs ,which track others’ mental states. Each belief type is generated via a separate LLM call, with prompts conditioned on current percepts, memory retrievals, and conversation history (see Appendix F). This modular belief formation feeds into the causal ToM template Figure 2a, which governs downstream action generation. Structured self-modeling thus serves as the foundation for cultural learning and perspective-taking.

4.3 Communication Medium 

To take advantage of representations of each other’s mental models and collaborate effectively, agents need to be able to communicate with each other. We operationalize communication in natural language, integrating the MIND FORGE framework’s communication system with Minecraft’s native in-game chat functionality. Specifically, once a MIND FORGE agent fails to complete a task, it initiates a communication round with its partner where it leverages its BigToM representation to ask for help. This system enables agents to exchange textual information and, crucially, to integrate feedback from collaborators directly into their belief systems. Concretely, we utilize the Mineflayer API [ 58 ] to extract and process chat data from the Minecraft environment. By leveraging the game’s existing chat system, the framework ensures that collaborative interactions occur in a context-appropriate manner, closely mirroring human-to-human communication within Minecraft. To handle the resulting conversation, we use a dedicated LLM instance within the MIND FORGE 

agent which is tasked with synthesizing and interpreting the conversation into the structured BigTOM BDI template, generating both interaction beliefs and partner beliefs (see prompts in Appendix F). This instance does not directly influence any other parts of the agent. 

4.4 Memory Subsystems 

A MIND FORGE agent’s memory consists of three subsystems drawing on research about human memory: (i) episodic , (ii) semantic , and (iii) procedural memory. 

Episodic memory deals with pertinent information about the agent’s past attempts at a task and social interactions with other agents. Recollections of past episodes serve as context for humans to interpret others’ behaviors, leading to divergent future actions in the same situation in response to different recollections [ 59 ]. Humans also tend to summarize information from their social inter-actions, but at the same time are capable of preferentially accessing episodes when the credibility 5of certain summaries is low due to the lack of evidence [ 60 ]. Moreover, findings also suggest that there are processes in the brain for updating these summaries as new episodes transpire [ 60 , 61 ]. Inspired by complementary learning systems theory, Larimar [ 62 ] enhances LLMs with a distributed episodic memory module, enabling dynamic knowledge updates without any gradient-based learning. Furthermore, Optimus-1 integrates past experiences in a multimodal memory storage mechanism and abstracted experience pool used for summarization of past experiences. For a MIND FORGE 

agent, the role of episodic memory is twofold. Firstly, it stores details about its partners revealed during previous conversations that can be exploited for perspective-taking in future communication. Secondly, we store past actions that led to failure and retrieve them based on their similarity with the task at hand. Thus, a MIND FORGE agent can track both the progress of its partner and learn from past failures. 

Semantic memory constitutes an individual’s store of knowledge about the world. The content of semantic memory is abstracted from the experience and is therefore said to hold summarized generalizations, likely without references to specific experiences [ 60 , 61 , 63 ]. For a MIND FORGE 

agent, the semantic memory stores information related to the task and environment. It stores information relevant to completing a task; for instance, the prerequisites for being able to mine dirt in the game world. Such a piece of information is not necessarily tied to one specific episode. Rather, it is generally useful information about the world and can be distilled from the episodic memory. Finally, procedural memory stores the skills that the agent learns and is analogous to the skill library present in the original Voyager framework [ 11 ]. The skills stored in the procedural memory are reusable pieces of code that the agent can use for solving problems, allowing the agent to take on more complex tasks while avoiding re-learning elements previously used successfully. As illustrated in Figure 2a, episodic and semantic memory stores influence the agent’s beliefs, while procedural memory provides the routines that inform the agent’s next action. Additional information about the memory subsystems can be found in Appendix B.2, where we provide implementation details, and an ablation for the episodic memory component, where we show that omitting it from MIND FORGE 

leads to a drop in success rate. 

5 Experiments 

5.1 Experimental Setup 

Our MIND FORGE framework introduces three advances that let agents improve continuously through social exchange: (1) explicit theory-of-mind representations for reasoning about peers, (2) Natural-language dialogue for coordination, and (3) integrated semantic and episodic memory to retain experience. We evaluate these additions in the same Minecraft world used by Voyager [ 11 ], built on MineDojo [ 64 ] with motor control via Mineflayer [ 58 ]. Component LLMs communicate through DSPy [ 65 , 66 ] and LangChain [ 67 ] for generation and embedding. In the base experimental setting, we follow Voyager [ 11 ] and allow the agent to execute 4 sequential actions to solve a task. MIND FORGE 

agents that interact (see Section 5.3) interleave one communication round (via the in-game chat) between two environmental actions. A snapshot of this setup can be seen in Figure 4a. For isolated tasks we measure the fraction of successes over 24 independent trials (one task per trial). In tech-tree experiments we follow Voyager’s protocol, tracking milestone achievements and unique items collected. To ensure comparability, we preserve Voyager’s original prompt structure and workflow throughout.

5.4 MIND FORGE Lifelong Learning 

We evaluate MIND FORGE ’s lifelong learning capabilities against both GPT-4 and open-weight Voyager [ 11 ] variants. The progression through increasingly complex technological achievements (from wooden to diamond tools) provides a rigorous test-bed for assessing lifelong learning and collaboration abilities of MIND FORGE . We follow the methodology from Voyager and evaluate performance on three key milestones: (1) wooden tools , (2) stone tools and (3) iron tools .In line with the findings in Table 1, Table 3 highlights the performance gap between GPT-4 powered Voyager and its open-weight variants, with only Llama 3.1-70B [ 71 ] reaching the wooden tools 

milestone. On the other hand, MIND FORGE shows substantial improvement across the board both in terms of achieved milestones and efficiency (number of iterations). Notably, Llama 3.1-70B [ 71 ]powered MIND FORGE achieves two additional tech-tree milestones, nearing the performance with the original GPT-4 powered Voyager [ 11 ]. We observe a steady increase in efficiency (number of iterations) as the size of the LLM increases: Llama 3.1-70B [ 71 ] powered MIND FORGE is 1.62 ×

more efficient than its Llama 3.1-8B counterpart for reaching the wooden tools milestone, suggesting 9that larger models can better utilize the collaborative learning framework. Furthermore, to understand the impact of collaboration in a mixed-expertise collaborative setting, we experiment with a non-collaborative MIND FORGE ablation. Notably, MIND FORGE agents without collaboration require on average 15 more prompting iterations to reach the wooden tools milestone and 34 iterations to reach the stone tools milestone. Thus, the collaborative framework not only enhances task completion rates but also enables more efficient acquisition of complex skills. We further test the generalization capabilities of MIND FORGE agents in Appendix A.2 where we show how even after collaboration finishes, the agents can reuse the knowledge in out-of-distribution tasks. 

5.5 MIND FORGE Collaborative Learning Setting 

While prior sections focused on instructive learning from stronger peers, we now consider a more challenging setup: collaboration between two MIND FORGE agents of matched capabilities , each powered by the same type of open-weight LLM. This setting reflects mutual cultural learning without access to an expert where the agents must bootstrap improvements solely through interaction, thereby mimicking certain qualities of emergence. Critically, we evaluate not individual trajectories, but instead focus on population-level performance : after N rounds of communication, what fraction of agent pairs from the total pool succeed in completing the task? As shown in Table 2, when agents begin from unprimed states, collaboration often leads to stagnation or even degradation which mirrors the “blind leading the blind” dynamic, where flawed mental models are mutually reinforced. However, even minimal prior expert instruction—such as a single round of GPT-4–guided collab-oration—can prime agents for more effective peer interaction, even if they still fail when acting alone. As shown in Figure 7, we pair Mixtral-8x7B agents that each received N communication rounds of GPT-4 guided trial before interacting. We observe that the fraction of successful agents steadily increases with additional communication rounds between themselves (Mixtral agents). From a 62% baseline population succeeding, we reach 79% of the population succeeding after seven turns, showing a 17% increment. This result reflects a form of test-time compute scaling, where richer interaction, not larger models, drives the emergent improvements across the group. This also aligns with the Condorcet Jury Theorem [ 73 ], which predicts that collective decisions improve when individuals exceed a baseline level of competence. Here, we see a distributed variant of this principle in action: once agents cross a minimal competence threshold, the communication induced bootstrapping can yield reliable gains at the population level. 

6 Conclusion 

The MIND FORGE framework demonstrates that cultural learning can overcome the limitations of open-weight LLMs in embodied environments. Integrating theory of mind, natural communication, and structured memory systems enables MIND FORGE agents to learn from both mixed and matched expertise peers and overcome fundamental failure modes in perception and code generation. Our experiments show that collaboration significantly improves performance on lifelong learning sce-narios, with agents collecting 2.3× more unique items and achieving 3× more tech-tree milestones when paired with expert agents. Crucially, in the collaborative setting, we show that the fraction of successful agents scales as a function of communication rounds; revealing emergent gains through perspective taking and interaction. These results suggest that social learning could reduce embodied AI’s dependence on large proprietary models, opening new paths for democratized agent development aligned with Green AI principles [74].

C.2 Episodic Memory 

We implement episodic memory as a Retrieval-Augmented Generation (RAG) using LangChain [ 67 ]. Specifically, we embed all the episodes where the agent failed to complete the task using 

text-embedding-ada-002 . An episode consists of the context used to generate the action, the code itself and the corresponding critic message. Subsequently, when the agent generates a new action, the RAG is queried to retrieve the k=5 most relevant failure episodes with respect to the task at hand. Lastly, to ensure we do not populate the context window with too many tokens, we generate a summary (using an LLM) of the retrieved episodes and feed this summary in the context used to generate the action. Below you can find the corresponding prompts for generating the episodic summary: System message You are a helpful assistant tasked with summarizing past experience episodes and pointing out the causes of failure. Create a concise summary. Prompt message Please summarize these episodes and why they failed:{combined_episodes} 

C.3 Procedural Memory 

The procedural memory has an identical design and functionality with the skill library introduced in Voyager [ 11 ]. Once a task gets successfully completed, we store the corresponding piece of code such that we can later reference it in adjacent tasks. This becomes increasingly important in tasks that require multiple steps, like crafting a pickaxe: the agent needs to first create a crafting table, create wooden sticks and only then attempt to craft the pickaxe. When we run the MIND FORGE framework in an open-ended setting, the procedural memory allows the agent to continuously evolve and create an increasingly large collection of reusable skills. 26 C.4 Semantic Memory 

As described in Section 4.4, semantic memory stores certain beliefs the agent has about the environ-ment, coming from experience with the environment and the other MIND FORGE agents. Whenever MIND FORGE attempts a new task, it creates a certain belief about how the task should be solved. For example, the agent might believe it requires tools to mine a block of dirt. This belief is then added to the context of the LLM when the environment action is generated. As highlighted in Section 1, one of the primary failure cases of Voyager are the false beliefs that come from this LLM generation. However, compared to Voyager [ 11 ], we leverage the communication with other MIND FORGE agents such that these beliefs get updated and refined. Below we showcase an example of false belief correction when trying to collect a block of wood: Initial belief inside the semantic memory {"How to mine 1 wood log in Minecraft?": "Answer: To mine 1 wood log in Minecraft, you need to use an axe."} Updated belief after communication {"How to mine 1 wood log in Minecraft?": "Answer: To mine 1 wood log in Minecraft, you need to punch a tree with your bare hands."} In addition to task-related memory, the MIND FORGE agent maintains a stateful set of beliefs about the other agents in the environment that are constantly updated across episodes. An example of this is presented in Figure 6, where the agent updates its representation based on the contents of the conversation. Thus, keeping track of various information and facts about other MIND FORGE agents acts as a semantic memory retrieval when agents communicate.

The specific models and their corresponding access methods are outlined below: • Llama 3.1-8B-Instruct : This model was accessed through the Lambda Inference API. 1 This API provided a reliable and efficient interface for interacting with the Llama 3.1-8B-Instruct model, enabling seamless integration within the M IND FORGE framework. • GPT-4 : Accessed via the OpenAI API.
