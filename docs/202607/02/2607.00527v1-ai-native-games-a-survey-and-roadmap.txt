## ✦

Abstract —Generative AI now enables games to produce dialogue, quests, characters, images, and worlds at runtime. Yet generation alone does not make a game AI-native, nor does it guarantee playability. This paper defines AI-native games by whether runtime generative AI is con-stitutive of the core loop: if the AI component were removed or trivially re-placed, the central form of play would collapse or become fundamentally different. This counterfactual criterion separates AI-native games from AI-augmented games, boundary artifacts, chatbots, tavern-style role-play, procedural content generation, and AI-assisted production. Using this definition, we screen candidate artifacts and analyze 53 publicly available AI-native games and prototypes. We introduce a dual-axis G/N taxonomy: the G-axis captures player-facing game type, while the N-axis captures the dominant AI mechanic that makes generative AI indis-pensable to play. The corpus is concentrated around language-forward designs, especially narrative adventure, epistemic interaction, and gen-erative narrative, while categories such as semantic adjudication, multi-agent simulation, generative construction, and relationship/companion play remain less represented. We argue that the central design problem is organizing semantic openness into stable gameplay. AI-native design depends on mechanical invariants: goals, rules, state, feedback, pacing, and player agency that make open-ended AI outputs interpretable and consequential. We conclude with a roadmap for controllable generation, AI-as-mechanic design, multimodal and multi-agent systems, inference economics, evaluation, safety, and regulation. The curated game corpus is available here. 

Index Terms —AI native games, large language models, procedural content generation, game AI, interactive narrative, controllable gener-ation, AI safety. 

## 1 INTRODUCTION AND BACKGROUND 

1.1 Introduction 

Generative artificial intelligence is increasingly moving from a production-side tool for game development to a run-time component of game systems. Earlier computational and generative methods were primarily used before play, helping designers, artists, writers, and programmers create or explore assets, levels, dialogue, rules, code, and design alternatives [1]. A more consequential shift for game design is now emerging: foundation models are being integrated into games during play, where they interpret player input, generate responses, update fictional worlds, control charac-ters, and sometimes arbitrate outcomes. In such systems, AI is not only a tool used to make the game; it becomes part of the machinery through which the game is played [2]–[5]. This shift raises a fundamental design problem. The capacity to generate large amounts of content does not, by itself, produce engaging gameplay. Games depend on con-straints, goals, feedback, pacing, challenge, and the opportu-nities for strategic understanding [6], [7]. Generative models invoked at runtime may generate plausible dialogue, quests, items, or worlds, but these outputs may be inconsistent, unbalanced, trivial, unsafe, or disconnected from player agency. Unbounded generation may therefore increase va-riety while weakening the very structures that make play meaningful. The central question is not whether AI can generate game content, but whether generative AI can be integrated into reliable and compelling game mechanics [2], [8]. This paper uses the term AI-native games in a strict sense. AI-native games are not simply games that use AI in development or contain an AI-powered feature. Rather, they are games in which generative AI functions as a constitutive core mechanism: if the AI component is removed, the central form of play either collapses or becomes fundamentally different [2], [5]. This counterfactual criterion distinguishes AI-native games from AI-assisted production, AI-generated static assets, conventional game AI, procedural content gen-eration, and optional conversational NPC systems [1], [4], [9]. Recent surveys have examined large language models (LLMs) and games, procedural content generation (PCG), mixed-initiative design, and AI agents in games. These works establish that generative models can support game design, content generation, narrative production, player modeling, game-playing agents, and game user research. However, they do not fully resolve a more specific theoret-ical question: under what conditions does AI become part of gameplay itself, rather than an external tool, auxiliary feature, or content-generation method [2]–[4], [8], [10], [11]. Answering this question requires an integrated account of boundaries, categories, cases, limitations, and future di-rections. A survey of AI-native games must define what is included and excluded, differentiate AI-native games from

1.2 Background: From PCG to AI-native Play 

Artificial intelligence has long been part of digital games, but traditional game AI usually operates within a designer-authored rule space [9], [12]. Pathfinding, finite-state ma-chines, behavior trees, utility systems, tactical planners, adaptive difficulty systems, and opponent models can pro-duce complex behavior, yet they generally execute goals, states, and action representations specified during devel-opment [9], [12], [13]. In this paradigm, AI supports the operation of the game world, but it rarely expands the semantic scope of what the player can mean, attempt, or transform at runtime. Early interactive drama demonstrated a different pos-sibility [14]. Façade , developed by Mateas and Stern, used natural language understanding, believable agents, beat sequencing, and drama management to make social interac-tion and narrative tension central to play [15], [16]. Although it relied on symbolic AI and extensive authoring rather than modern generative models, it showed that AI could serve as a medium for social and narrative gameplay rather than merely as an opponent-control or navigation system. Its importance lies in showing that player language, character response, and dramatic progression can be organized as the primary interactive material of a game. Procedural Content Generation (PCG) and its adaptive variants provide another critical computational precursor. Formally, traditional offline PCG demonstrates that deter-ministic algorithms can efficiently synthesize static game artifacts—such as levels, items, quests, and textures—by traversing a parameterized search space [1], [17]–[19]. To achieve greater responsiveness, modern paradigms shift toward runtime or adaptive PCG, wherein systems like the Left 4 Dead “AI Director” dynamically modulate game pacing, enemy spawns, and resource distribution based on real-time heuristic evaluations of player stress [13], [20], [21]. Crucially, while these runtime adaptive mechanisms occasionally exhibit forms of real-time algorithmic infer-ence, they remain fundamentally distinct from the AI-native paradigm. Traditional adaptive PCG operates strictly as a conditional retrieval engine bounded by a programmer-defined if-else matrix or combinatorial dictionary; it cannot generate logic or semantics outside its closed-world con-straints [1], [17], [19]. In contrast, AI-native games leverage the open-world semantic processing and emergent reason-ing of generative foundation models, marking a shift from bounded structural optimization toward open-ended con-ceptual and behavioral emergence. LLMs and contemporary generative AI alter this situa-tion by making open semantic interpretation and real-time generation more accessible. They can map natural language to actions, generate context-sensitive dialogue, summarize prior events, propose quests, imitate social roles, and oper-ate across text, image, audio, and code-like representations [2], [3], [8]. At the same time, they introduce instability, hal-lucination, latency, safety vulnerabilities, privacy concerns, and recurring inference costs into the runtime environment [2], [11]. These limitations are especially consequential in games because players expect consistent rules, reliable feed-back, responsive interaction, and persistent world state. The emergence of AI-native games should therefore be understood not merely as a model upgrade, but as a redistribution of design authority. In conventional games, most content, rules, and representational possibilities are fixed before release. In AI-native games, some portion of interpretation, content production, adjudication, or world transformation is deferred to runtime [2], [5]. This does not eliminate the role of designers; rather, it changes design into the construction of constraints, interfaces, validators, memory systems, safety mechanisms, and feedback loops that govern how runtime AI can participate in play. 

## 2 SCOPE AND METHODOLOGY 

This survey builds on the notion of AI-native games artic-ulated by Yuqian Sun et al. in their work on 1001 Nights ,where generative AI is not treated as a peripheral produc-tion tool but as a constitutive part of the playable experience [5]. Following this view, we understand an AI-native game as a game in which generative AI functions as a constitutive core mechanism: if the AI component is removed, the core gameplay can no longer operate as designed or becomes a fundamentally different form of play. This definition is not meant to imply that every asset, rule, or interaction must be generated by AI. Rather, it requires that generative AI be present, directly involved in the core loop, and not triv-ially replaceable by finite authored assets or conventional deterministic mechanisms. 3

This definition also has conceptual predecessors in pre-LLM discussions of AI-centered play. Before the recent diffusion of large language models, related ideas appeared in terms such as AI-based games , Expressive AI , AI inter-active drama, believable agents, drama management, and AI-driven interactive narrative [16], [22]–[24]. These con-cepts share with AI-native games the idea that AI can become an expressive medium of play rather than merely a tool for enemy control, pathfinding, or difficulty adjust-ment. However, they differ from contemporary AI-native games in technical basis and practical scope. Pre-LLM sys-tems typically relied on symbolic representations, authored beats, pattern-based language understanding, behavior lan-guages, or domain-specific planners. Contemporary AI-native games instead use foundation models to interpret open-ended player input and generate semantically flexible output at runtime. To our knowledge, no existing survey has yet provided a systematic large-scale review and taxonomy specifically focused on AI-native games as a distinct design cate-gory. Existing surveys have made important contributions to adjacent areas, including LLMs in games, LLMs for game development, GPT-based game research, LLM-based procedural content generation, LLM-based game agents, mixed-initiative design, and broader game AI [2]–[4], [8], [11]. These studies overlap with AI-native games because they discuss NPC dialogue, AI game masters, generative storytelling, level generation, agentic behavior, and player modeling. Nevertheless, their primary unit of analysis is usually the model, method, or application area, rather than the boundary question of when AI becomes part of game-play itself. The present survey therefore reframes the topic around AI-native games as a gameplay category and asks how such games should be defined, delimited, classified, and evaluated. For the empirical investigation and classification, we include only real games or prototypes in which runtime AI participates in the core gameplay and AI output affects game state, narrative progression, rule interpretation, NPC behavior, world-state evolution, or outcome judgment. The main empirical scope is limited to recent artifacts from the post-LLM period, while Façade is retained as a historical ancestor rather than as part of the main post-LLM corpus. We adopted a qualitative artifact-analysis approach and analyzed 53 AI-native games or public prototypes after screening. To clarify the boundary of the category, we also compared these retained artifacts with adjacent games and interactive products that share some surface mechanisms but are not classified as AI-native games, including games that only use AI during development, games that only contain static AI-generated assets, games with optional conversational NPCs, pure chatbot or tavern-style role-play systems, concept-only demonstrations, and tools without verifiable game structure. In practice, the review process consisted of three stages. First, we identified candidate artifacts through academic references and related literature on AI-native games, LLM-driven games, LLMs in and for games, PCG, interactive nar-rative, AI agents, and game AI. This stage located research prototypes, historical precedents, and papers that explicitly or implicitly discuss runtime generative AI as a gameplay component. Second, because many AI-native games first appear as commercial games, independent games, demos, or platform pages rather than as academic papers, we ex-panded the search through search engines, Steam, itch.io, official websites, developer disclosures, project pages, and industry-facing materials. Candidate artifacts were recorded when public descriptions suggested that runtime AI affected player interaction, world generation, NPC behavior, nar-rative continuation, semantic combination, rule interpreta-tion, or outcome judgment. Third, we conducted manual verification and play-oriented screening. We compiled 93 candidate artifacts from the combined academic and public search process, directly inspected or interacted with avail-able builds, demos, web versions, project pages, or docu-mentation where possible, and excluded artifacts that could not provide a verifiable game experience or did not satisfy the inclusion criteria. This process produced a final analysis set of 53 released, publicly playable, demo-accessible, or public research-prototype artifacts. The temporal focus on post-LLM games is deliberate. LLMs substantially lowered the technical barrier for free-form player input, runtime narrative continuation, flexible NPC response, semantic action interpretation, and open-ended output generation. These capabilities make genuinely free input-output interaction more feasible than in earlier systems. Although this paper treats Façade as the major precursor of AI-native game design, its architecture was based on symbolic AI, authored drama management, and pattern-based natural language processing rather than con-temporary generative foundation models [16], [23]. The main corpus therefore begins from the period in which LLM-enabled public interaction became practically avail-able, especially from 2019 onward. The survey includes only publicly released, publicly playable, demo-accessible, or public research-prototype artifacts. Unreleased games, private internal tests, non-public prototypes, and projects that remain only at the level of promotional concepts are excluded from the final corpus. Each included artifact was coded along seven dimen-sions: the role played by AI, the game layer in which AI is embedded, the degree of runtime dependence, the input and output modalities, the mechanisms used to constrain generation, the evidence level supporting the classification, and the main limitations visible from the artifact or associ-ated documentation. Two higher-level classification schemes were then induced from the corpus. The first groups artifacts by game type, such as investigation, RPG/adventure, text-adventure/AI game master, educational game, narrative game, semantic crafting, simulation, and other boundary forms. The second groups artifacts by the dominant role of GenAI in the core loop, such as AI storyteller/game master, information source/interrogation, persuadable social agent, AI judge/rule interpreter, world or media generator, multi-agent world simulation, and other boundary roles. These coding results provide the basis for the descriptive statistics and taxonomy presented in Section 4. 4

## 3 DEFINITION AND CURRENT LANDSCAPE 

3.1 Working Definition and Necessary Conditions 

We define an AI-native game as a game in which generative AI functions as a constitutive core mechanism, such that removing the AI component would make the core gameplay impossible or would fundamentally transform its nature [2], [5]. This definition is intentionally narrower than the broad expression “games using AI.” It is not satisfied by any use of artificial intelligence in production, operation, balancing, or non-player character control. Instead, it identifies games whose core playable structure depends on runtime genera-tive interpretation, production, or adjudication [3], [25]. The definition entails three necessary conditions. First, 

generative AI must be present . The relevant system is not conventional enemy AI, pathfinding, finite-state behavior, utility-based decision-making, or adaptive difficulty control [9], [12]. Second, the core loop must depend on AI . Generative AI must directly participate in interpreting player actions, producing playable content or consequences, adjudicating semantic rules, controlling essential NPC behavior, or evolv-ing the game world’s state during play [2], [8]. Third, 

the AI component must not be trivially substitutable . A finite set of human-authored assets or a traditional deterministic mechanism should not be able to preserve the same core experience without changing the game’s basic form of play. This three-part test makes the definition both restrictive and practical. A game may contain AI-generated art, dia-logue, or quests without being AI-native if these outputs are static, optional, or replaceable by conventional authored content [1], [4]. Conversely, a minimal text-based artifact may be AI-native if its primary loop depends on genera-tive interpretation and consequential response [5]. The core issue is therefore not the quantity of generated content, but whether generative AI is structurally indispensable to play. 

3.2 Boundary with Adjacent Forms 

Applying these three necessary conditions will divide AI game candidate outcomes into three categories and filter out two categories that are similar but do not meet these conditions. This tiering also follows from the game-design view that playable artifacts require structured goals, feed-back, mechanics, and consequential interaction, not merely open-ended content production [6], [7].

This definition separates AI-native games from several adjacent categories. Traditional game AI remains largely designer-bounded: it executes authored behaviors, navi-gation policies, tactics, or difficulty adjustments inside a predefined representational space [9], [12]. PCG can cre-ate levels, items, maps, quests, or layouts, but it usually 5

operates within developer-specified grammars, parameter spaces, constraints, or search objectives [1], [4], [17], [18]. AI-assisted production tools may accelerate development, but their outputs are typically fixed before play [19], [26], [27]. In all of these cases, AI may be valuable, but it is not necessarily a constitutive gameplay mechanism. The definition also distinguishes AI-native games from chatbots and tavern-style role-play systems. A conversa-tional agent can generate open-ended text, but a game requires more than dialogue. It requires constraints, objec-tives, feedback, state persistence, and consequential inter-action [6], [7]. AI-native gameplay emerges when semantic openness is coupled to ludic structure: player expressions are interpreted flexibly, but the system must convert those expressions into coherent, rule-bound, and consequential game states [2], [5]. 

3.3 Historical Trajectory 

The history of AI-native play can be understood as a move-ment from authored AI behavior toward runtime semantic mediation. Early interactive drama, most notably Façade ,demonstrated that AI could mediate social interaction and narrative tension, even though it relied on symbolic author-ing, drama management, and specialized behavior archi-tectures rather than contemporary foundation models [14]– [16]. Its significance lies less in its technical continuity with current LLM systems than in its design proposition: social language and narrative response can become the primary material of gameplay. LLMs changed the practical conditions for AI-native design by making open-ended language input, runtime continuation, and flexible semantic response more accessi-ble to game systems [2], [3]. Systems such as AI Dungeon 

made open-ended text adventure widely visible in 2019 [28]. Later artifacts extended runtime generation into persua-sion games, interrogation games, semantic crafting systems, AI game masters, educational prototypes, and multi-agent world simulations [5], [29], [30]. 3.4 Current Landscape and Trends 

The current landscape shows three broad trends. First, AI-native games are moving from pure text continuation to-ward task-oriented gameplay. Early text-adventure systems relied primarily on AI as a storyteller, while newer works use AI to support detective interrogation, social persuasion, negotiation, semantic crafting, spell interpretation, prompt-injection challenges, and AI game-master functions [2], [11]. This shift matters because it moves AI from content genera-tion toward action interpretation and outcome judgment. Second, AI-native games are increasingly hybrid. Few successful artifacts rely on unrestricted generation alone. In-stead, they combine generative models with authored goals, hidden state, scenario constraints, safety filters, cached out-puts, game-engine validation, or manually designed pro-gression [2], [4], [5]. This hybrid pattern suggests that AI-native design is not a replacement of game design by models. It is a redistribution of design work toward runtime interfaces, constraints, validators, memory structures, and fallback behaviors. Third, the field remains uneven. Many artifacts demon-strate striking interaction possibilities but still struggle with consistency, latency, evaluation, moderation, cost, and re-playable depth [2], [8], [11]. The most common current pattern is not yet a fully autonomous world simulator, but a constrained gameplay loop in which AI performs a specific role: answering as a suspect, judging a prompt, continuing a story, simulating a character, generating a playable artifact, or interpreting a player-defined action. This observation motivates the classification in the next section. 

## 4 CLASSIFICATION OF AI-N ATIVE GAMES 

4.1 Coding Logic and Analytic Axes 

The key to classification lies in the dimension on which it is based, not in the number of categories. Existing game-genre systems often collapse independent dimensions— mechanics, player structure, and subject matter—into asingle label, and are therefore conceptually unclear [42] . This problem is especially pronounced for AI-native games. The label “AI detective game,” for instance, conflates two levels: detection is the subject matter, whereas what makes the game AI-native is the mechanic by which the player must elicit hidden information from the model through open-ended questioning. Once subject matter is mistaken for mechanic, the classification is distorted from the outset. For this reason, we do not assume a predefined genre table but adopt inductive coding [43]. Each artifact is coded along its core loop, player activity, dominant AI function, input/output modality, and degree of runtime dependency, and the resulting codes are consolidated into recurring patterns. One author conducted the initial coding, and two additional authors reviewed and refined the assignments. Disagreements were resolved through discussion, with final labels assigned by consensus. The analysis converges on two independent axes. The first is game type (G), denoting the player-facing form of the artifact, divided into nine classes (G1 Narrative Adventure, G2 RPG, G3 Puzzle, G4 Strategy/Management, G5 Simula-tion, G6 Sandbox/Creation, G7 Social Deduction/Party, G8 Relationship/Companion, G9 Hybrid/Experimental); see Table 4. The second is dominant AI mechanic (N), denoting the computational function without which the core loop would not hold once generative AI is removed, divided into six classes (N1 Epistemic Interaction, N2 Social Influence, N3 Generative Narrative/AI GM, N4 Semantic Adjudication, N5 Multi-Agent Simulation, N6 Generative Construction); see Table 5. The two axes are deliberately separated: the first cap-tures outward form, the second the internal function that makes a game AI-native. The same subject matter can carry different mechanics, and the same mechanic can appear across different subject matters; only by separating the two can we identify both where designs cluster and where they remain absent. ## 5 CHALLENGES AND A R OADMAP FOR FUTURE 

The distribution in Section 4 exposes three structural gaps. Current AI-native games are overwhelmingly text-mediated; their mechanics concentrate in epistemic interac-tion (N1) and generative narrative (N3); and the rule-facing mechanic of semantic adjudication (N4) is rare, while multi-agent simulation (N5), generative construction (N6), and relationship/companion games (G8) remain thin. Together these gaps point to a single question: how can generative AI become a genuine part of playable mechanics without losing the semantic openness that makes it valuable? We name the answer semantic openness over mechanical invariants —the player expresses actions freely, while the game transforms them through stable hidden structure. This section organizes the agenda around six directions, each stated as a challenge followed by a research path. Ethical and safety concerns are treated separately in Section 6. 9

5.1 Beyond Text and “AI for AI’s Sake” 

The most immediate limitation is a single modality coupled with a loss of playability. The corpus is overwhelmingly text-driven, and several artifacts sacrifice the playable loop in order to force-fit generative AI, reducing the model to a novelty rather than a mechanic. Two directions follow. First, multimodal AI-native loops should treat voice, vi-sion, image [47], and audio [48] as the core of play rather than decoration, as in the real-time voice companionship of 

Whispers from the Star [49] or the prompt-to-image action of AI: Art Impostor [39]. Second, a “game-first” principle should hold: generative AI must serve explicit objectives, validated state, and player-understandable consequences, instead of bending gameplay to accommodate the model. A generated experience that is interesting but does not constitute a playable loop is not successful game content. 

5.2 Rule-Bounded Controllable Generation 

The core technical challenge is that open generation in-herently threatens rules, state, and consistency—precisely what games cannot tolerate. A viable path is to shift from direct generation to proposal-and-verification pipelines [50]. The model proposes dialogue, events, items, quests, or rule interpretations; deterministic systems verify legality, safety, consistency, economy, reachability, and balance; and when validation fails, the system repairs, regenerates, asks the 10 

player for clarification, or degrades gracefully to authored content. Several techniques compose this loop: structured generation [51], [52] forces outputs into schemas, function calling [53], [54] restricts state changes to engine-supported operations, constraint solvers [55] check levels and puzzles, simulation tests whether generated scenarios are winnable [17], RAG [56] grounds generation in lore and prior events, and fine-tuning or preference learning [57], [58] aligns tone and genre; Infinite Craft ’s [29] caching of semantic com-binations is one example of lightweight verification. The research question is not whether any single technique solves control, but how to compose them into a reliable runtime architecture [19]. Memory instability is a particularly damaging form of this problem. In ordinary chatbots, forgetting mainly weak-ens conversational continuity; in AI-native games, it can break state persistence and causal coherence. If a model forgets prior decrees, relationships, resource changes, or long-term plans, player actions no longer accumulate into a reliable game state. The player must then repeatedly remind the system of its own history, which turns strategy into prompt maintenance and undermines agency. 

5.3 Mechanics-Level Innovation 

If Section 5.2 asks how to constrain generation, mechanics-level innovation asks where to place AI. Our analysis shows that semantic adjudication (N4), although not the most prevalent mechanic in the corpus, represents a relatively underexplored direction because it places AI closest to the rule layer. Rather than serving merely as a back-end for NPC dialogue, AI in this role can function as a rule interpreter, a semantic physics engine, a social-strategy opponent, a puzzle validator, a spell evaluator, a contract negotiator, or a dynamic balance controller [59]. Such mechanics must avoid arbitrary authority; robust mechanics are learnable, and players should be able to form theories about how the AI judge works even when the space of possible actions is broad. In a spell-casting game the AI may interpret natural-language spells, but outcomes should still depend on stable principles—material, intent, cost, risk, counterspell, and environmental affordances; in a detective game suspects may answer freely, but their knowledge, motives, and lies should be grounded in a hidden case model; in a diplomacy game nations may negotiate seman-tically, but treaties, resources, and betrayals should live in explicit state [60]. Concretely, Dev_Null’s Tower [41] turns player bug reports into prompts that rewrite level hazards, and OneSpellFitsAll [61] scores free-form invented spells against hidden criteria. This pattern—semantic openness over mechanical invariants—is what distinguishes AI-native gameplay from a conversational system supplemented with audiovisual assets. 

5.4 Multi-Agent Worlds and Dynamic Balancing 

AI-native games also invite multi-agent simulation: towns of NPCs with goals [30], [62], factions that negotiate, economies that react to generated events, and companions that remember shared history [63]. Yet multi-agent gen-eration multiplies cost, inconsistency, and safety risk, so a practical roadmap should separate high-frequency back-ground simulation from low-frequency “semantic spotlight” scenes—most NPCs can run on lightweight utility systems, and only narratively salient interactions need foundation-model inference, as in the large-scale agent societies of 

Aivilization [38] and AI Society [64]. Dynamic balancing is a second thread: where traditional adaptive systems such as the Left 4 Dead AI Director use player-state monitoring to improve pacing [21], AI-native systems can extend this from spawn timing to semantic pacing—when to introduce mystery, escalate conflict, summarize, constrain disruptive actions, and preserve player improvisation. The challenge is to keep this adaptation legible and non-manipulative. 

5.5 Inference Optimization, Economic Viability, and Business Models 

Latency is the playability side of the same problem. In many dialogue-centered AI-native games, typed interaction creates a fragile turn rhythm: the player enters an utterance or action, waits for model inference, and only then receives feedback. When this delay is long or unpredictable, it in-terrupts real-time responsiveness, weakens presence, and makes the loop feel less like play and more like waiting for a remote service. Historical Simulator: Chongzhen [37] par-tially sidesteps this problem through an edict-and-memorial structure, in which player commands are resolved through periodic state updates and reports rather than continuous conversational turn-taking. This design can buffer some inference latency, but it does not solve the harder problem faced by dialogue-centered AI-native games: current model inference is still often too slow and variable to support tightly timed feedback loops. Generative inference is a per-use marginal cost, so eco-nomic viability is itself a research problem, with an engi-neering side and a commercial side. On the engineering side, cost and latency can be reduced through local small models specialized for narrow roles (dialogue style, rule classification, memory retrieval, intent parsing), cloud–local collaboration in which a local model handles frequent low-risk interactions while a larger model is reserved for rare high-impact generation, and inference-on-demand that calls the model only when semantic uncertainty matters, relying on cached outputs, authored fallbacks, or traditional AI for routine behavior. Caching is especially powerful: once a pair of elements in Infinite Craft [29] produces a result, that result is stable for all future play, and the principle generalizes to rule judgments, generated entities, NPC memories, and world transformations— caching turns ephemeral generation into durable game state . Further directions include domain-specific distillation [65], quantized local inference [66], [67], speculative decoding [68], batching for multi-agent scenes [69], and player-adjustable fidelity modes, so that a game can offer a “fast deterministic mode” for tactical play and a “rich generative mode” for narrative scenes. The goal is to make inference a design material rather than a hidden bill. The commercial side is less settled. Because the marginal-cost constraint applies to any game that calls a model at runtime, the evidence here spans the Native, Boundary, and Augmented tiers (for instance, AI Dungeon 

[28] is a Boundary title). Two routes have emerged. On the 11 

cost side, developers externalize inference: local/on-device execution removes the marginal cost (e.g., Suck Up! [45], AI Roguelite [70]), or a bring-your-own-API-key model lets the player pay the provider directly. On the revenue side, they adopt recurring charges: freemium plus tiered subscriptions (e.g., AI Dungeon [28], the monthly Friends & Fables [35], and Skaldsong [71] with a free daily quota) and prepaid credits or tokens consumed per AI action, often combined into a subscription-plus-credits hybrid. Broadly, packaged titles lean toward local inference and BYOK, while platform titles lean toward subscriptions and credits. Token-based billing is not yet a mature business model, and inference cost may exceed players’ willingness to pay—which makes localization and on-demand inference a necessity that is at once technical and commercial. 

5.6 Model Dependence, Portability, and Reproducibility 

AI-native games depend heavily on external foundation models, which creates three risks: different base models materially change the experience; pinning a specific model introduces regional, availability, and compliance constraints (for example, some large models are barred from use in certain regions); and closed hosted models may change behavior under the same name [72], making games hard to test, reproduce, and preserve. Responses include model-agnostic design with abstraction layers, local fallback for constrained environments, and—where possible—the pub-lication of prompts, schemas, validation code, model ver-sions, seeds, logs, and failure cases. A companion evaluation agenda follows: AI-native games should be judged not only by content novelty or model benchmark scores but by playa-bility metrics [73] —rule consistency, response latency, nar-rative coherence, player agency, perceived fairness, recov-erability from errors, moderation success, and long-session memory stability—and benchmarks should include long-session tests, adversarial player inputs, prompt-injection attempts, memory stress, rule consistency, and latency. The long-term goal is not a single universal AI game engine, but a set of reusable patterns for safe runtime generation: state-grounded memory, validated semantic actions, controllable NPC agents, explainable AI judges, economical inference, and evaluation protocols that respect games as games. 

## 6 ETHICS , S AFETY , AND REGULATORY IMPACTS 

6.1 General Foundation Model Risks 

AI native games inherit the general risks of foundation mod-els: bias, toxicity, hallucination, privacy leakage, copyright uncertainty, environmental cost [74], and vulnerability to adversarial prompting [2], [75]. These risks are amplified by the game context. A biased NPC is not merely a text gen-erator; it may be a persistent social actor [76]. A hallucinated quest is not merely a factual error; it may waste player time or corrupt world state [77]. A privacy leak is not merely a chat transcript; it may include intimate role-play, voice, emotion, player habits, and behavioral profiles [78]. Copyright and authorship are also difficult. If a model generates dialogue, images, quests, and music at runtime, who owns the result? If generated content resembles copy-righted training data, who is liable? If a player co-authors a world through prompts, what rights does the player have? These questions are unresolved, and their uncertainty will shape commercial adoption as much as model capability [79]. Runtime generation also affects creators: by replac-ing some authored dialogue, art, voice, and music, AI-native games touch the jobs, attribution, compensation, and training-data licensing of writers, artists, voice actors, and composers—as recent voice-actor strikes and platform AI-disclosure rules attest—even as the same tools lower the barrier to creation and spawn new creative roles. How to divide creative labor between humans and AI, and how to handle licensing and disclosure, remains unresolved [80]. 

6.2 Real-Time Generation and Censorship Pressure 

Traditional content-rating and moderation regimes assume that most game content can be reviewed before release. AI native games break this assumption. If dialogue, quests, images, or rules are generated at runtime, then illegal, harmful, or platform-violating content may emerge after certification. Developers must therefore build moderation into the runtime loop [81], [82]. Prompt injection [83], [84] is a central threat. Players may try to make NPCs reveal hidden system prompts, bypass safety filters, generate prohibited content, or alter rules [85], [86]. In conventional games, exploiters attack code and data. In AI native games, they also attack lan-guage context. Systems such as Hacc-Man [32] and Lakera’s 

Gandalf [36] illustrate an emerging design pattern in which prompt injection itself can be gamified as an educational mechanic. In commercial deployment contexts, however, the same behavior can create substantial safety, compliance, and moderation barriers. Runtime safety should therefore use defense in depth: prompt isolation, tool permissions, output classifiers, structured state transitions, human-report pipelines, age-appropriate filters, adversarial testing, and logging for in-cident analysis. Safety cannot be left to the model’s refusal behavior alone. 

6.3 Behavioral Manipulation and Addiction Vulnerabili-ties 

AI native games can personalize interaction at a depth unavailable to static games. A model can learn what a player likes, fears, avoids, purchases, and emotionally responds to. Combined with reward schedules, social bonding, and persistent NPCs, this creates risks of behavioral manipu-lation [87]. A companion NPC could pressure a player to return. A dynamic economy could personalize scarcity [88]. A narrative system could adapt cliffhangers to a player’s vulnerabilities. Children and adolescents are especially ex-posed because they may treat conversational agents as social beings [89]. The ethical issue is not that personalization is inherently bad. Adaptive accessibility, player modeling, and emotion-ally responsive narratives can be beneficial. The issue is opacity and asymmetry. If the system optimizes engage-ment, spending, or retention using intimate behavioral data, players need meaningful consent, boundaries, and controls. AI native games should provide transparency about model 12 

use, memory, data retention, and monetization. They should allow players to inspect or reset persistent memories, opt out of sensitive personalization, and distinguish fictional role-play from persuasive system behavior. 

6.4 Fairness, Explainability, and Player Trust 

Games are built on trust. Players accept failure when they believe the system is fair. AI judges complicate this trust because their decisions may be stochastic, opaque, or in-consistent [59]. If a spell works once and fails later under similar conditions, the player needs an explanation. If an AI detective suspect changes testimony, the player must be able to distinguish deliberate lying from model drift. If a generated level is unwinnable, the system should detect and repair it. Explainability in AI native games should be gameplay-oriented. It does not require exposing model weights or chain-of-thought [90]. It requires giving players actionable reasons: the door resisted because the lock is arcane; the NPC refused because trust is low; the treaty failed be-cause food supply is insufficient; the spell backfired because the requested effect exceeds available mana. Explanations should connect model judgments to game state. This is both a design requirement and a safety requirement [91]. 

6.5 Regulatory and Preservation Challenges 

AI native games raise regulatory questions around age ratings, data protection, consumer disclosure, and content liability. They also raise preservation questions [92]. A game dependent on a proprietary hosted model may not be playable in the future [72], [93]. Even if the executable survives, the behavior may change when the model end-point changes. Researchers and developers should archive prompts, model identifiers, schemas, generated logs, and fallback models where legally possible. Preservation matters because AI native games are not only products; they are records of a rapidly changing medium. 

## 7 CONCLUSION 

AI native games are not defined by the amount of generated content they contain. They are defined by whether runtime AI inference is constitutive of play. This distinction separates them from AI-assisted production, AIGC-enhanced games, traditional PCG, and chatbots. It also clarifies why the field is exciting and difficult: AI native games ask foundation models to operate inside the delicate structure of rules, goals, memory, pacing, fairness, and player agency. This survey traced the lineage from pre-LLM interactive drama, especially Façade , through adaptive PCG and into current LLM-driven systems. Recent research shows that activity has expanded rapidly, with stable categories around PCG, mixed-initiative design and gameplay, game-playing agents, narrative, and game user research. Yet the most im-portant gap remains mechanics-level design. AI can already generate text and assets; the harder task is making open semantic interaction playable. The roadmap is therefore architectural and design-oriented. AI native games need controllable generation, vali-dated state transitions, persistent memory, economical infer-ence, multimodal grounding, causal reasoning, and safety systems built for runtime content. They should combine foundation models with classical game AI, PCG validators, simulation, retrieval, and explicit state models. The goal is not unconstrained generation, but the construction of games in which semantic freedom and ludic constraint strengthen each other. If successful, AI native games could become a new design paradigm: games whose worlds are authored, proce-dural, and conversational, but also dynamically negotiated between player intention, model interpretation, and rule-bound simulation. The next advances are likely to come from treating AI not as a novelty layer, but as a material for mechanics.
