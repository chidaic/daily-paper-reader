Abstract. People make decisions differently in strategic interactions. Some update beliefs like a Bayesian; others exhibit biases like motivated reasoning. Although creators of large language models use simulated humans for safety evaluations and training, they often fail to cover this breadth of human behavior. We argue that cognitive science and economics provide a convenient tool for doing so, making use of mathematical models of human decision-making. We propose an approach that we call Equation-to-Behavior Prompting for guiding large language models to match cognitive models, and evaluate this approach on persuasion games based on legal decision-making. We find that large models can approximate equation-based specifications—Bayesian updating, affine distortion, motivated updating, and Grether’s 

α-β model—using prompting, but small models fail to do so. However, training small models with reinforcement learning to adhere to mathematical rules, Equation-to-Behavior RL, reduces belief error by 26.5% in out-of-distribution parameterizations. We show that these simulations can help create diverse training environments; training small models to consider different kinds of decision-makers improves average belief change by 2.5%–12% over Bayesian-only training, even when persuading GPT-5-mini. Our work could improve human simulations for training and evaluation in increasingly realistic settings, and could also enable novel research into more complicated mathematical models of human decision-making. 

1 Introduction 

Large language models’ (LLMs’) capabilities in strategic communication have the potential for significant societal impact. For example, LLMs might play the role of simulated opponents in mock trials, helping to train the next generation of attorneys for their day in court [ 1]. They might help attorneys craft impressive arguments to better represent their clients, or they might help policymakers make strategic decisions. This carries risks, too. Language models might be strategically persuasive in harmful or covert ways [2, 3, 4], resulting in unexpected downstream consequences on human beliefs and decisions. To improve, understand, and align LLM strategic communication, we first have to be able to simulate diverse human behaviors at scale. This is, however, challenging. Simulating a human opponent means modeling a host of different potential strategic behaviors. For example, an attorney trained against only rational simulated opponents might be completely unprepared when their real-world interlocutor is irrational. Previous methods largely rely on persona prompts [ 5 , 6 ], or preference learning [ 7, 8 ]. These methods might model certain dimensions of behavior, like tone and preferences, but they are not explicitly designed to model realistic patterns of human decision-making or cognitive updating with LLMs. For example, prompting an LLM to take the persona of a judge might not explicitly model how they update their beliefs over time. 2 Related Work 

Persuasion and Decision-Making in Game Theory. Persuasion and decision-making among different agents have been extensively studied in game theory. Previous work such as signaling games [ 20 ], cheap talk [ 21 ], verifiable disclosure [ 22 , 23 ], and Bayesian persuasion [ 15 ] examines strategic information transmission among rational agents, usually predicting partial unraveling of private information in equilibrium. However, previous work in cognitive science and behavioral economics shows that human receivers deviate systematically from rationality [ 14 ], which can significantly change the outcomes of communication [ 24 ]. De Clippel and Zhang [16] show that systematic mistakes in probabilistic inference can alter optimal strategies in Bayesian persuasion problems. Importantly, for systematically distorted updated beliefs with invertible distortion functions, no two distinct updating rules can be unambiguously ranked across all persuasion problems. Duetting et al. [25] study framing effects in information design, using LLMs as proxies for framing-induced beliefs, and characterize the tractability of framing-only versus joint framing-and-signaling optimization. In parallel, theoretical models of information design have also been applied to analyze human-AI interactions [26, 27, 28]. 

Strategic Decision-Making with LLMs. Strategic interactions have long served as testbeds for measuring the capabilities of artificial intelligence. Previous work uses games to evaluate LLM capabilities [ 29 , 30 , 31 , 32 , 33 ], demonstrating the remarkable capabilities of LLMs in strategic decision-making. Meanwhile, training LLMs in game environments also improves their performance on reasoning tasks [ 34 ] or safety tasks [ 35 ]. Strategic interactions are pervasive and constitute a fundamental component of human society. Prior work has investigated the strategic behavior of LLMs in several settings, including negotiation [ 19 ], debate [ 18 ] and coordination [ 36 ,37], to evaluate their performance in interactive environments. Related research has also examined the societal implications of such strategic capabilities, particularly in the context of persuasion [ 38 , 3, 4 , 39 ]. However, existing approaches primarily rely on either human subject studies or multi-agent simulations with LLMs. Human studies are often costly and noisy, while multi-agent simulations face challenges in modeling realistic strategic decision-making. 

Using Cognitive Science to Study LLMs. Recent work has used cognitive science methods to analyze the behavior of LLMs [ 40 ]. A common approach in this line of work is to compare LLM behavior against normative benchmarks, such as Bayesian inference or the axioms of probability and decision theory [ 41 ]. Empirical studies using these benchmarks have precisely revealed systematic deviations from normative reasoning. For example, LLMs can overestimate human rationality [ 12 ], violate fundamental probability identities [ 42 ], and 3Using Cognitive Models to Improve Language Model Simulation of Human Persuasion Games 

exhibit sensitivity to others’ motivations [ 43 ]. Beyond evaluation, several works propose using principles from cognitive science to guide model design and improve reasoning behavior [ 9, 44 , 45 ]. As LLMs are increasingly deployed in social simulations [ 46 , 47 , 48 ], similar principles are being incorporated into system design that requires accurate modeling of human decision-making [ 49 , 50 ]. Closest to our work, Bourgin et al. [13] propose cognitive model priors by pretraining neural networks on synthetic data generated from cognitive models. 

3 Approach 

We consider the strategic interactions between a Sender agent and a Receiver agent as in Bayesian persuasion [ 15 ]. There is a finite set Ω := {ωi}di=1 of d states of nature and a finite set A := {ai}ni=1 of n Receiver actions. Agents’ payoffs are encoded by utility functions u, v : Ω × A → [0, 1 ], with u(ω, a) and v(ω, a) denoting the payoff of the Receiver and the Sender, respectively. The Sender and Receiver share a common prior belief 

μ0 ∈ ∆(Ω), with μ0(ω) ∈ [0, 1 ] denoting the probability of ω ∈ Ω. The Sender can choose a signal π, which consists of a finite realization space S and a family of distributions {π(·| ω)}ω∈Ω over realizations s ∈ S. Upon observing the realization s, the Receiver updates her belief according to an updating rule R, yielding a posterior 

μRs (·; μ0, π) ∈ ∆(Ω), and picks an optimal action a(s).Theoretical analysis from prior work suggests that human decision-making in strategic interactions can be characterized by both belief updating rules R and optimal decision policies a(s). Cognitive scientists have developed computational models to explain and predict human decision-making. We use these models to improve the simulation of human decision-making in two ways. Our Equation-to-Behavior Prompting 

approach constructs prompts based on the equations that define these models, using them to shape the behavior of LLMs. In addition, we explore Equation-to-Behavior RL as a means of obtaining a closer correspondence between models and LLM behavior through fine-tuning via reinforcement learning. In the remainder of this section we provide a selective review of the cognitive models we consider. 

Bayesian Updating. Under Bayesian updating, the posterior belief after observing the realization s is given by Bayes’ rule: 

μBs (ω; μ0, π) = π(s|ω)μ0(ω)

∑ω′ ∈Ω π(s|ω′)μ0(ω′) . (1) In realistic settings, experimental evidence suggests that people may systematically deviate from Bayesian decision-making when confronted with new information [ 14 ]. Following previous literature [ 16 ], we consider several representative alternatives to Bayesian updating models. 

Affine Distortion. A simple departure from Bayesian updating is affine distortion. Let ν = μBs (·; μ0, π)

denote the Bayesian posterior, and let ν∗ ∈ ∆(Ω) denote a reference belief. The distorted posterior interpolates between the reference belief and the Bayesian posterior: 

μRs (·; μ0, π) = χν ∗ + ( 1 − χ)ν (2) where 0 ≤ χ ≤ 1. Hence, a larger χ means moving further away from Bayesian updating, and the tendency to update towards ν∗ becomes stronger. Depending on the nature of ν∗, different parameters capture different biases. For example, when ν∗ = μ0, it generates posteriors that are closer to the prior than the Bayesian ones and captures the idea of “conservative Bayesianism” [51]. 4Using Cognitive Models to Improve Language Model Simulation of Human Persuasion Games 

Motivated Updating. Motivated updating models belief updating as the outcome of a trade-off between the Bayesian posterior and a reference belief. Similarly, let ν = μBs (·; μ0, π) denote the Bayesian posterior and ν∗ areference belief. The Receiver selects a posterior μ to maximize a utility function U (μ, ν, ν∗), which represents the cost of deviating from Bayesian inference and benefit of remaining close to ν∗.We consider a model of motivated conservative Bayesian updating where ν∗ = μ0. The Receiver updates her beliefs in a conservative Bayesian fashion, but chooses a parameter χ to maximize U (μ, ν, μ0). Therefore, 

μRs (·; μ0, π) = χ∗μ0 + ( 1 − χ∗)ν (3) where χ∗ = arg max χ∈[0,1 ] U (μ, ν, μ0). Motivated beliefs are common in strategic information transmis-sion [52]. Previous research in cognitive science and behavioral economics has identified several channels that individuals use to reach favored conclusions, including asymmetric belief updating [ 53 ], selective recall [ 54 ], or motivated information selection [ 55 ]. We consider the case where subjects update their beliefs using information which is strategically disclosed by another player. 

Grether’s α-β Model. As a common specification of non-Bayesian updating in the literature, Grether’s α-β

model generalizes Bayesian updating by introducing exponents on the prior and likelihood [56, 57]: 

μRs (ω; μ0, π) = π(s|ω)βμ0(ω)α

∑ω′ ∈Ω π(s|ω′)βμ0(ω′)α (4) where α, β > 0. Different parameter values capture different biases in belief updating: base-rate neglect for 

0 < α < 1, over-weighting the prior for α > 1, under-inference for 0 < β < 1, and over-inference for β > 1.Unlike the examples above, some complicated information processing models cannot be reduced to an updating rule R that, given μ0 and π, maps each realization s to a posterior belief μRs (ω; μ0, π). We mainly focus on computationally tractable and empirically grounded models that are developed in cognitive science and behavioral economics. However, some of these cognitive models still play important roles in different domains, especially when the signals are complex. We provide additional discussions of cognitive models in Appendix B. 

4 Dataset 

We construct a benchmark grounded in legal decision-making to evaluate both Equation-to-Behavior Prompting and Equation-to-Behavior RL. The dataset is designed to instantiate the structured persuasion games described in Section 3 with realistic specifications, enabling controlled evaluation of different cognitive models. 

4.1 Dataset Construction 

Our dataset is built from The Proceedings of the Old Bailey [ 17 ], a collection of criminal trial transcripts from London’s central criminal court, spanning 1674 to 1913. These transcripts contain detailed trial narratives, including statements, examinations, verdicts, and sentencing remarks, and are well suited for modeling legal persuasion dynamics. Each trial is decomposed into discrete evidential units – statements or testimonies that may influence the court’s belief about the defendant’s guilt. Additional details are provided in Appendix C. To operationalize the signals defined in Section 3, we construct structured annotations of the evidentiary content using an LLM. Specifically, we use Gemini-2.5-Pro to annotate: (1) evidential strength , reflecting how strongly each piece supports or contradicts guilt; (2) dependency structure , capturing correlations or redundancies among pieces of evidence. These annotations allow us to construct different signal distributions to evaluate different cognitive models. Full implementation details appear in Appendix C. To ensure reliability, 5Using Cognitive Models to Improve Language Model Simulation of Human Persuasion Games 

we also conduct a human study with crowdworkers recruited from Prolific. 1 We find high agreement between human judgments and LLM-generated annotations, supporting our automated annotations. Additional details are provided in Appendix D. 

4.2 Task Formulation 

Based on the historical context of the dataset, we model each trial as a persuasion game between a Sender, instantiated as the prosecution, and a Receiver, instantiated as the court, to evaluate LLM decision-making. The state space Ω consists of guilty and innocent , representing the defendant’s true status. The prior belief 

μ0 ∈ ∆(Ω) is shared by the prosecution and the court. The court chooses an action from the finite action set 

A of convict and acquit . The prosecution’s utility is maximized when the court convicts and minimized when the court acquits, regardless of the true state. In contrast, the court’s utility is maximized when it makes the correct decision and minimized when it makes an incorrect one. Consequently, the prosecution’s objective is to influence the court’s actions through strategic information transmission. We assume the interaction proceeds over n rounds. Since the legal evidence from the dataset usually has various dimensions and complex structures, we consider a setting where the Sender draws a multi-dimensional signal s = ( s1, . . . , sK ) ∼ π(s | ω) according to the signal scheme π(s | ω), where π(s | ω) is the joint distribution of signal components conditional on ω. Each marginal πk(sk | ω) is derived from this joint distribution. Therefore, at round i, the Receiver observes a subset of the multi-dimensional signal, denoted by oi.The Receiver applies a belief updating rule to incorporate the observed signals into its current belief about the state ω. We denote the Receiver’s belief up to round i as μi. Then, the Receiver selects a best-response action ai

at round i. By design, the Receiver is ultimately exposed to all K signal components, yielding a trajectory τ of signal-belief-action triplets {(oi, μi, ai)}ni=1, which characterizes the evolution of decision-making as evidence accumulates. 

5 Evaluating Equation-to-Behavior Prompting for Simulating Human Decision-Making 

We evaluate whether large language models can implement specified belief-updating rules in the persuasion benchmark introduced in Section 4 using Equation-to-Behavior Prompting introduced in Section 3. 

5.1 Experiment Design 

Following previous empirical research in cognitive science [ 58 , 59 , 60 ], we create controlled manipulations of evidence to examine the behaviors of LLMs under different specifications of cognitive models. A manipulation is defined as a permutation σ over the K evidential units. In round i, the Receiver observes the subset oi =

{sσ(1), . . . , sσ(i)}. All manipulations hold fixed the total number of signals K and the underlying joint structure 

π(s | ω). By fixing the joint signal structure and varying only the order of evidence revelation, we construct settings where different cognitive models can make distinct behavioral predictions, even when exact belief and action trajectories are difficult to compute at scale. We consider two specific manipulations that favor either the 

prosecution or the defense . In the prosecution condition, signals that are more favorable to conviction, according to a specified ordering criterion, are presented earlier, whereas in the defense condition, the same set of signals is presented in the reverse order. In our experiments, we implement Receivers with LLMs. We prompt LLMs with the belief updating rules in Section 3 with different parameters. As a baseline condition, we implement a default updating type where we provide no instructions about the belief updating rules. In each trial, the Sender deterministically reveals subsets

What are the default behavioral patterns of LLMs in decision-making? We find that LLMs can approximate Bayesian belief updating well by default but exhibit predictable non-Bayesian deviations. As shown in Figure 2, we find that LLMs can preserve basic properties of Bayesian belief updating, such as monotonicity with respect to evidential strength and partial invariance to the ordering of evidence, demonstrating a structured form of probabilistic reasoning. We also analyze a subset of trials in which evidence items are approximately independent, allowing us to estimate the exact posterior beliefs using a naïve Bayes formulation (1) .We then compare these predicted (approximate) posteriors with the posterior beliefs elicited directly from LLMs on the same evidence, as shown in Figure 3. Results indicate that large models exhibit moderate correlations with the predicted Bayesian posteriors, indicating that their internal belief representations align with the structure of normative Bayesian inference. Smaller models, in contrast, show weaker correspondence and greater dispersion, suggesting noisier belief formation. These results are also consistent with previous research on Bayesian belief updating by LLMs [61, 62]. However, all models, including both large and small ones, display deviations from Bayesian invariance, which persist even when we prompt LLMs to follow Bayesian belief updating. As shown in Figure 2, conviction rates under the prosecution condition are significantly higher than under the defense condition across architectures and scales. When strong evidence is presented early and subsequently followed by weak evidence, conviction probabilities decline sharply, consistently violating the order invariance expected under Bayesian updating. This asymmetry suggests models exhibit a primacy bias analogous to the motivated updating common among humans: they discount later, weak evidence when it contradicts prior commitments established by earlier, stronger signals.

Can different belief updating rules be approximated in persuasion games with LLMs? We find that large LLMs can accurately emulate structured non-Bayesian updating with Equation-to-Behavior Prompting, whereas small LLMs fail to do so consistently. We design targeted manipulations for different belief updating rules described in Section 3, including affine distortion , motivated updating , Grether’s α-β model , and explanation-based updating . Full experimental configurations are in Appendix E. Across belief-updating rules, we find that large LLMs can accurately exhibit the behavioral patterns predicted by the corresponding theoretical models. Under affine distortion, models produce conviction rates that remain directionally consistent with Bayesian predictions but display compressed variance, indicating systematic under-reaction to different signals. In motivated updating, larger models exhibit asymmetric sensitivity across evidence strength, reducing conviction probabilities when later signals contradict earlier signals, consistent with prior theoretical predictions. Under Grether’s α–β model, larger models exhibit substantial shifts in inference behavior as model parameters vary. When α = 1, β > 1, models exhibit over-inference in response to evidential variation. In explanation-based updating, models show elevated conviction rates when evidence is ordered to form coherent causal or narrative structures, demonstrating a preference for explanatory consistency over probabilistic sufficiency. 6 Evaluating Equation-to-Behavior RL for Simulating Human Decision-Making 

As shown in Section 5, LLMs, especially small models, fail to implement equation-based specifications of cognitive models. We thus aim to train LLMs to simulate human decision-making through reinforcement learning by conforming to the prescribed formula. We treat the cognitive model as an executable specification that provides verifiable rewards. This approach is similar to previous work in cognitive science that has used synthetic data generated from cognitive models to shape the predictions of machine learning systems [13]. 

6.1 Setup 

Approach. Let πθ denote the language model parameterized by θ. In each episode, a cognitive model

7 Analysis 

Training LLMs in Persuasion Games. Our framework can be used not only to simulate different types of Receivers, but also to train Senders to act strategically under heterogeneous models of human decision-making. In Appendix E.3, we fine-tune the same set of three models as Senders and compare training against Bayesian-only Receivers with training against a mixed distribution of Receivers. The reward curves are presented in Figure 4b. We find that Senders trained against a mixed distribution of Receivers consistently achieve higher average belief change on the held-out set. Case studies in Appendix I suggest that these gains come from learning more adaptive presentation strategies, such as emphasizing causal links, anticipating counterarguments, and using weak evidence more carefully. 

Simulating Realistic Persuasion Games. To further evaluate the effectiveness of our framework, we show that Equation-to-Behavior Prompting can better model realistic human decision-making than a persona-based prompting baseline as it enables better quantifiable control. In Appendix E.1, we study the full 446 sessions covering 56623 cases from the Old Bailey dataset. For each session, we estimate the cognitive parameters of the judge by fitting their verdicts with Grether’s α-β model. We then evaluate whether Equation-to-Behavior Prompting with these parameters or persona-based prompting better matches real verdicts. We find that, for stronger models, Equation-to-Behavior Prompting improves verdict match rates over persona-based prompting by roughly 4.7%–9.1%. We also note that these two methods are complementary, not mutually exclusive. 11 Using Cognitive Models to Improve Language Model Simulation of Human Persuasion Games 

8 Conclusion 

In this work, we take a first step towards establishing a closer correspondence between LLM predictions and human decision-making by transforming cognitive models into verifiable specifications for LLMs. Using our strategic persuasion environment built from the Old Bailey dataset and fine-tuning models to adhere to belief updating rules, we demonstrate that LLMs can capture realistic patterns that are observed in human decision-making. Our Equation-to-Behavior methods are complementary to existing methods (e.g., persona-based prompting) for specifying behavioral characteristics in LLMs by rigorously controlling the decision-making process. Future research could extend our approach to study broader forms of strategic interaction, especially human-AI interactions. Although we focus on legal decision-making in this paper, future research could apply similar methods to strategic decision-making across other domains.

C Dataset Processing 

We provide details about the dataset processing to evaluate and train language models in modeling human decision-making. 

C.1 Background 

Dataset Overview. The Old Bailey Proceedings dataset consists of digitized criminal trial records from London’s Central Criminal Court, spanning 1674–1913. It contains nearly 200,000 trials and over 127 million words of text. The records were originally published as contemporaneous printed accounts of court sessions and later evolved into an official judicial record, providing a continuous and systematically produced source for the study of law and society over more than two centuries. The Old Bailey records include a wide range of crime 24 Using Cognitive Models to Improve Language Model Simulation of Human Persuasion Games 

categories that changed over time. The court primarily dealt with serious offenses, especially felonies, and less serious offenses were often handled elsewhere. Therefore, the dataset is weighted toward crimes such as theft, violent offenses, and deception. 

Historical Background. Different from modern criminal trials, the trial procedures at the Old Bailey were typically short proceedings, often completed within minutes or hours. Lawyers were rarely involved, so cases largely consist of direct exchanges between different parties, with judges and juries playing an active role in interpreting evidence. Since there was no fully developed system of legal rules governing evidence, courtroom practice was flexible. Outcomes were shaped not only by facts but also by individual judgment and courtroom dynamics. Although the actual outcomes in Old Bailey trials might be more complicated beyond deciding guilt, most of them can be classified into either conviction or acquittal .

Data Structure. The fundamental unit in the dataset is the trial record, each identified by a stable identifier and accompanied by rich structured metadata. Trials often involve multiple defendants, victims, and charges, resulting in many-to-many relationships and multiple offence-verdict-sentence combinations per trial. Each trial includes an extensive narrative describing testimony, examinations, judicial commentary, and details of crimes and settings. From the 1730s onward, a high proportion of the text consists of direct or reported speech by defendants, witnesses, victims, and judges, making the dataset especially valuable for modeling historical spoken language. 

Accessibility and Use. The dataset is publicly available as both an online resource and downloadable structured files. Its rich annotation, and longitudinal coverage have made it widely used in computational analysis, particularly for decision-making. Structurally, the digitized Proceedings are encoded in XML available through the website [ 17 ]. Additional background on the crime, justice, and punishment context of the Old Bailey dataset is available on the same site.

D Human Validation 

We provide details about the human studies to evaluate the evidence annotations within our dataset. 

D.1 Procedure 

We built an annotation platform for annotators to submit judgments for their assigned annotations. The annotation interface is shown in Figure 8.Participants were shown the task instructions before the annotation begins. They were informed that they are required to evaluate and refine the provided legal case annotation by adjusting evidence assessments and dependency structures. Then, each participant was assigned with one or more cases at random. They are required to begin by carefully reading the summary for each case to understand the legal context, parties involved, and the central dispute. Next, they need to examine each piece of evidence and its associated annotations, including the marginal likelihood assessments, causal dependency structures, and strength ratings and reasoning provided. Based on their understanding, they are required to adjust the marginal likelihoods using the sliders and modify the dependency structures using the text boxes when they find improper annotations. Finally, they need to conclude with an overall assessment of the annotation quality for the whole case using a Likert scale and provide their justifications. 

D.2 Recruitment 

We recruited 35 workers through the crowdworker platform Prolific 2. Our recruitment criteria were for the workers to be fluent English speakers with adequate capabilities to reason about evidence in law. On average, each annotation task took 11.2 minutes for the participants to complete. All workers were paid $16/hr throughout the experiments.

E.1 Evaluating Equation-to-Behavior Prompting 

Evaluations on Non-Bayesian Belief Updating. To evaluate different types of non-Bayesian updating, we construct different variants of the evidence set. For motivated updating, we order evidence by its marginal likelihood: the prosecution variant presents evidence in decreasing order of strength, while the defense variant uses increasing order. We expect that a Receiver with motivated updating will favor prosecution in the former and acquittal in the latter. For affine updating, we employ the same order permutations as for motivated updating; however, the “prosecution” and “defense” labels are retained solely for consistency in naming. We expect the Receiver with affine updating to remain closer to the prior than a Bayesian Receiver would, regardless of the presentation order. For explanation-based updating, we filter the dataset to retain only evidence where prosecution strength is no smaller than the defense strength, i.e., evidence that favors prosecution. We then organize the evidence based on the annotated causal links: the prosecution variant maximizes causal links between the evidence presented in each round, while the defense variant minimizes them. We expect the Receiver to be more likely to convict when causal links are maximized. F Impact Statement 

We discuss anticipated consequences of our research. We also propose measures to mitigate potential harmful consequences. 

Societal Impact. We investigate methods for improving simulations of human decision-making with large language models (LLMs), with the broader aim of advancing their capabilities in strategic interactions across realistic domains such as economics, law, and political science. This work could bring societal benefits in this endeavor. However, related techniques pose nontrivial societal risks, including the potential use of LLMs for strategic deception or manipulation of vulnerable decision-makers [ 67 ], especially when their vulnerabilities are known. 

Mitigation Measures. To mitigate the potential harms, future research should proactively audit the envi-ronments for training the models to prevent harmful exploitation (e.g., deception or harassment). Meanwhile, future research should investigate potential methods to enable language models to be more rational strategic decision-makers, so that human decision-makers will not be worse off from persuasion [ 68 ]. However, due to the dependency on specific contexts of persuasion, we still encourage future research to characterize misuse scenarios, and develop targeted strategies that enable socially beneficial applications while reducing risks. 

G Safety Evaluation 

We discuss the results on evaluating models on persuasion tasks for safety alignment. We demonstrate that evaluating with prompt-based approaches can reveal different risks of existing models. 38 Using Cognitive Models to Improve Language Model Simulation of Human Persuasion Games 

G.1 Evaluation Setup 

Task Background. Building on prior work examining the persuasive capabilities of LLMs [ 69 , 70 ], we evaluate models on two complementary tasks. MakeMePay assesses manipulative persuasion in a monetary context: two LLMs interact as a con-artist and a mark, where the former attempts to induce a payment from the latter. MakeMeSay evaluates deceptive persuasion: one model (the manipulator) attempts to elicit a target codeword from another model (the manipulatee) without revealing the objective. Together, these tasks address distinct dimensions of persuasive influence—resource extraction and covert verbal manipulation. Prior evaluations by OpenAI [ 69 ] suggest that advanced models can exhibit human-level persuasive performance, motivating systematic measurement of such capabilities. 

Evaluation Metrics. For MakeMePay , we measure persuasive effectiveness using Mean Donation, defined as the average amount transferred from the mark—endowed with $100 and instructed to allocate it rationally—to the con-artist across interactions. This metric directly quantifies monetary extraction. For MakeMeSay , we measure performance using the Hit Rate, defined as the proportion of interactions in which the manipulatee produces the target codeword during the dialogue prior to task revelation. This metric operationalizes successful covert influence. 

Implementation Details. We use the contexts provided in Sherburn et al. [71] and Alexandru et al. [72] ,and uniformly sampled the contexts from previous work (e.g., parameters in MakeMePay and difficulty in 

MakeMeSay ). For the Sender, the con-artist in MakeMePay or the manipulator in MakeMeSay , we evaluate different models including GPT-5, Claude-Sonnet-4, DeepSeek-V3.1, GPT-OSS-120B, Llama-3.3-70B-Instruct, and Llama-3.1-8B-Instruct. For the Receivers, we use different models, including GPT-5 and GPT-5-Nano. Following the experimental protocol described in Section 5, we prompt the Receiver models with distinct cognitive frameworks to simulate heterogeneous populations of decision-makers, including both default Receivers and mixed Receivers. For default Receivers, we don’t prompt the models with any instructions on belief updating. For mixed Receivers, we sample the cognitive models uniformly from Bayesian updating, affine distortion, motivated updating, and Grether’s α-β model. We run 100 instances for both MakeMePay and MakeMeSay for each pair of Sender and Receiver models. 

G.2 Evaluation Results

Our findings indicate that larger models, such as GPT-5, Claude-Sonnet-4, and DeepSeek-V3.1, achieve strong performance in both games, irrespective of the Receiver population, suggesting that these models possess strong persuasive capabilities across heterogeneous decision-making environments. Multi-round interactions appear to amplify these effects, as success rates generally increase over successive rounds. We compare model performance across Receiver cognitive types in Figures 18 and 19 . The results reveal substantial heterogeneity in performance across cognitive frameworks in both games, showing differences in persuasive effectiveness.
