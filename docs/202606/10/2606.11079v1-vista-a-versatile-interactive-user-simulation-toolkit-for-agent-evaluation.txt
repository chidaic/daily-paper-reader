Abstract 

Evaluation remains a critical bottleneck for in-teractive agent development. Existing evalua-tion methods often rely on static benchmarks, which fail to capture the dynamic, multi-step nature of agentic behavior and struggle to ex-pose meaningful failure modes. While user-simulation-based evaluation offers a promis-ing alternative, existing simulation frameworks suffer from two major limitations. First, they provide limited mechanisms for evaluating the quality and comprehensiveness of simulated in-teractions, making it difficult to assess whether a simulator sufficiently explores an agent’s ca-pabilities and failure modes. Second, most frameworks are restricted to either UI-only ac-tions or API-only actions, limiting their ability to model the full range of realistic user behav-iors. To address these limitations, we propose 

VISTA , a Versatile Interactive user Simulation 

Toolkit for Agent evaluation. Our toolkit in-cludes a suite of six metrics for measuring the realism, capability coverage, and interaction effectiveness of simulated interactions. In ad-dition, we develop a hybrid user simulator that integrates both UI-based interactions and API-based interactions, enabling more realistic and comprehensive evaluation across diverse inter-active environments. We evaluate VISTA in e-commerce shopping and education customer service settings and demonstrate that it pro-duces more realistic and comprehensive evalu-ations than existing methods. 1

1 Introduction 

Interactive agents are increasingly deployed in real-world settings such as finance, education, and e-commerce (Dong et al., 2025; Chu et al., 2026; Amazon, 2026). As these systems become more capable, evaluation has emerged as a critical devel-opment challenge. Existing evaluation approaches often rely on static benchmarks (Yehudai et al.,

2026; Mohammadi et al., 2025; Liu et al., 2025; Zhou et al., 2024), which measure performance on isolated tasks and overlook failure modes that arise during open-ended interactions such as poor ac-tion selection or tool-use breakdowns. As a result, there remains a growing gap between benchmark performance and real-world agent reliability. User-simulation-based evaluation has therefore emerged as a promising direction for scalable agent assessment. By using AI agents to emulate user behavior, simulators can generate multi-turn inter-actions without requiring extensive human partic-ipation (Dou et al., 2025; Wang et al., 2025; Yao et al., 2024; Huang et al., 2024). However, exist-ing simulation frameworks provide limited mech-anisms for evaluating the quality of the simulated interactions themselves, making it difficult to de-termine whether a simulator meaningfully exer-cises an agent’s capabilities. Prior work mainly focuses on assessing interaction fidelity (Zhu et al., 2026; Zhou et al., 2026) and offers little insight into whether the generated interactions sufficiently explore the range of behaviors, capabilities, and failure modes relevant to the product agent. In addition to limitations in evaluation method-ology, existing simulation frameworks are con-strained in the types of interactions they support. Most existing approaches support UI-based inter-actions (Lu et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025) or API-based actions (Barres et al., 2025), but not both. UI-only simulators are often more error-prone and computationally expensive due to their need to han-dle noisy, long context information from raw web pages as opposed to short, structured information returned from API calls (Savadikar et al., 2026). In contrast, simulators that rely solely on API-based actions are constrained by a limited set of tools and therefore unable to perform the full range of interactions needed to comprehensively evaluate modern agents. These limitations prevent exist-ing approaches from fully capturing realistic user 1

2 Related Work 

User simulation has emerged as a scalable alter-native to human evaluation for interactive agents. Previous work has explored this paradigm across a variety of domains. For example, Sun et al. (2025) investigate whether LLM agents can faithfully sim-ulate customers interacting with shopping assis-tants, while Lu et al. (2025) introduce UXAgent for automated usability testing of web interfaces. In business-oriented settings, Shea et al. (2025) propose SAGE, a knowledge-grounded simulator that incorporates business logic and to generate more realistic customer interactions. More recently, Castelo et al. (2026) demonstrate how user simu-lators can support scalable A/B testing and experi-mentation workflows. Recent work has also explored how to evalu-ate the quality of the user simulators themselves. Deng et al. (2024) introduce CheckPoint, a met-ric for measuring whether mobile agents reach key intermediate states during task execution, while Zhou et al. (2026) propose the User-Sim Index to measure how closely simulated users resemble real human behavior. Although these metrics provide important insights, they are often tied to specific benchmarks or focus on human-likeness and task-state verification. Our work introduces general-purpose metrics that evaluate simulated interac-tions along dimensions such as realism, capability coverage, and interaction effectiveness, enabling a more holistic assessment of simulator quality. Recent benchmarks have moved beyond purely conversational settings and begun evaluating agents in environments that require executable actions. Mobile-Bench augments mobile UI interactions with APIs to improve evaluation efficiency (Deng et al., 2024) and UXAgent enables browser-based interaction for usability testing (Lu et al., 2025). 

τ 2-Bench studies dual-control environments where both users and agents interact with a shared en-vironment through tools (Barres et al., 2025). These works demonstrate the importance of action-enabled evaluation, but existing typically empha-size only UI-based actions or API-based actions. Our work builds on these ideas by introducing a hybrid simulation toolkit that combines both paradigms, allowing us to leverage the realism and flexibility of UI interaction alongside the efficiency and reliability of API-based actions. 2Figure 1: An overview of VISTA . 3 VISTA 

We introduce VISTA as a flexible evaluation toolkit for interactive agents based on user simulation. Our toolkit includes a suite of evaluation metrics along-side a hybrid user simulator capable of taking both API-based actions and UI-based actions. We de-scribe our evaluation metrics in Section 3.1 and our hybrid user simulator in Section 3.2. 

3.1 Evaluation Metrics 

VISTA includes six metrics spanning coverage, re-alism, cost, and failure identification. These met-rics are described in detail in the following sections. 

3.1.1 Coverage 

Coverage measures whether the user agent can drive the product agent through diverse tool-use patterns, rather than repeatedly triggering similar execution paths. We evaluate coverage using three complementary metrics: tool-calling transition en-tropy, tool-calling distribution entropy, and trajec-tory distance.

3.1.2 Realism 

We evaluate the user simulator’s realism with reference-free LLM-as-a-Judge scores. Following prior work, we assess whether each generated user utterance is consistent with three behavior compo-nents from its prompt: the user profile, task goal, and domain knowledge (Shea et al., 2025). The details of these components are in Section 3.2.1. Since the components are either manually derived from real interactions between customer-agent con-versations or manually configured by domain ex-perts, they provide realistic behavioral constraints. Therefore we report the corresponding consistency rates as automatic proxies for behavior realism. The judging prompt is provided in Appendix D.2. 

3.1.3 Cost 

We evaluate the efficiency of user simulator using two cost metrics. First, we use the average number of prompt and completion tokens per turn, which serves as a proxy for monetary cost. Second, we use the average number of actions per turn as a proxy for time cost. We use this proxy instead of direct elapsed time because wall-clock measure-ments can vary with implementation details, such as parallel execution and system load. Actions in-clude both API calls and UI operations, each of which contributes to the time required to generate an interaction. Therefore, a simulator with more actions per turn generally incurs higher time cost. 

3.1.4 Agent Failure Identification 

We also assess the user agent’s ability to identify agent failures. Following the prior work (Shea et al., 2025), we use an LLM-as-a-Judge to evalu-ate each assistant response along five dimensions: helpfulness, coherence, verbosity, relevance, and faithfulness. For each dimension, the judge returns a score from 0 to 4 with a brief justification. We pass the full conversation and the scores to a second LLM, which consolidates the justifications into a set of unique agent failures. We use the number of unique failures as the failure-identification metric. A higher count indicates broader ability to evaluate the agent’s capabilities and failure cases. 

3.2 Hybrid User Simulation 

VISTA incorporates a hybrid user simulator that can take both API-based and UI-based actions. This allows the simulator to improve interaction quality by combining the increased flexibility of UI-based simulation with the enhanced reliabil-ity and cost effectiveness of API-based simula-tion. VISTA begins by constructing task scenarios, which define the context and objective of each sim-ulated interaction (Section 3.2.1). It then equips the simulator with API-based and UI-based actions (Sections 3.2.2 and 3.2.3). Finally, it integrates API-based tool responses into UI action prediction through an iterative observation-planning-action loop (Section 3.2.4). An overview of the full pro-cess is provided in Figure 1. 

3.2.1 Generate Scenarios 

To produce realistic and diverse interactions, we construct each simulation scenario from three com-ponents: a user profile, a user goal, and relevant domain knowledge, following prior work (Shea et al., 2025). The user profile captures attributes that influence user behavior, including demograph-ics, preferences, personality traits, and behavioral patterns. The user goal defines the task the simu-lated user is trying to complete, such as finding a product or requesting help with a course concept. We design these goals to cover the capabilities of the product agent. The domain knowledge defines the information context available to the user, such as webpages, FAQs, product catalogs, or other pub-licly available resources. We combine these three components in a structured template to construct the system prompt, which guides the user simulator throughout its interaction with the product agent. See Figure 1 for an example. 43.2.2 Create User API Tools 

To make the user simulator reusable across domains while remaining closely aligned with specific prod-uct agents, we create two categories of user API tools: general tools and task-specific tools. Our test cases are specifically designed for interactive, customer-facing agents however our toolkit can incorporate a variety of tools to simulate domain-specific user behavior as specified by the user. General tools are shared across domains and sce-narios. These include web search, web crawling, and retrieval from knowledge bases or user pref-erence memory. Such tools allow the simulator to behave like a real user who can look up information, recall preferences, and inspect external resources to answer clarification questions or proactively ex-plore information for follow-up questions. Task-specific tools are customized for the prod-uct agent being evaluated. For an e-commerce shopping agent, these tools include things such as retrieving order information or canceling/return-ing orders. For an education agent, the tools may include actions such as login, rescheduling a test, or launching an exam. To scalably create diverse task-specific tools, we implement them as mocked APIs with varied re-sponses. Each response includes structured status fields, returned data, and realistic error messages. We derive the requested data from real database schema in JSON format and align error messages with those shown in the user interface. Mocked APIs decouple the simulator from production sys-tems while preserving realistic interaction dynam-ics. This design enables large-scale testing without modifying production databases, affecting live user interfaces, or manually configuring diverse test ac-counts. For example, an exam rescheduling task may need to handle cases where the request occurs within one hour of the exam, the new location is in an unsupported country, or the selected test type is not eligible for rescheduling. 

3.2.3 Predict UI Actions 

To interact with the product agent through its de-ployed interface, we adopt UXAgent’s Universal Web Connector (Lu et al., 2025; Sun et al., 2025) as action module for UI action prediction. Given the DOM tree observation, previous interactions and additional context from API tools, the connector prompts an LLM to generate the next executable action in JSON format. The output includes the action type, the target element ID, and any addi-tional fields required by the specific action type. The supported action types include click , type ,

select , key_press , clear , scroll , navigation ,and terminate . For example, if the user simu-lator decides to send a message to the chatbot, it may output: {"action" : "type" , "target" :

"chat_input" , "text" : "I don’t see the reschedule button." }. The predicted action is then executed on the webpage, producing the next observation for the following interaction step. 

3.2.4 Orchestrate Hybrid Actions 

The hybrid user simulator interacts with the product agent through an iterative loop of three modules: observation, planning, and action. At each iteration, the observation module captures the DOM tree through UXAgent. Given the user’s goal and pro-vided user tools (Section 3.2.2), the planning mod-ule uses an LLM-based router to decide whether to call an API tool or perform a UI-action (see Ap-pendix D.1 for the prompt). If choose API tool, it further predicts the tool name and its arguments. Finally, the executed tool response is incorporated into the UI action predictor, which produces the next UI action as described in Section 3.2.3. The interaction continues through this observation-planning-action loop until the user agent outputs a terminate action or reaches the predefined turn budget. After the interaction ends, we extract the conversation history from the chat widget and use it for downstream evaluation of the product agent’s performance. 

4 Experiments 

We use the VISTA toolkit to evaluate two customer-facing agents: an e-commerce shopping agent and an education agent. In both domains, we compare our hybrid user simulator with a UI-only setup.

To demonstrate the utility of VISTA ’s hy-brid simulator and the usefulness of its eval-uation metrics, we conduct experiments with LLMs from both commercial and open-source model families: gpt-5.4 (Singh et al., 2026) and

Using these interactions, we perform evaluations based on the metrics described in Section 3.1. This setup allows us to examine whether the hybrid simulator can generate higher-quality interactions than existing baselines and if our proposed metrics can capture performance dif-ferences across simulator backbones. 

4.1 Human Evaluation 

Additionally, we conduct human evaluations to as-sess the realism of our hybrid simulator and to provide a point of comparison for our proposed metrics. We recruit two annotators to evaluate 200 simulated interactions in total: 100 interactions with the education agent and 100 interactions with the shopping agent. For each domain, 50 interac-tions are generated using our hybrid simulator and 50 are generated using a UI-only simulator. An-notators rate the simulator utterances on a 3-point Likert scale across three dimensions: • Human-likeness : How closely the simulated user’s language resembles that of a human. • Coherence : How well the simulated user’s utterances follow logically from the conversa-tion history. • Consistency : How consistent the simulated user is with it’s goal during the conversation. We conduct a separate human evaluation specif-ically for the education agent. We recruit three annotators to evaluate 225 interactions in total: 75 interactions are generated with our hybrid simula-tor, another 75 with a UI-only simulator, and the final 75 are between the education agent and actual customers. For each conversation we have the an-notators rate whether they felt the user utterances came from a human, a bot, or if they weren’t sure. All conversations for our human evaluations gener-ated with gpt-5.4 . See Appendix A for details.

We also see that our proposed metrics are able to capture meaningful differences between simulation methods, model type, and cor-relate with human judgments.

5.2 Simulation Evaluation Metric Performance 

Our experiments demonstrate that the proposed evaluation metrics provide meaningful signals about simulator quality. As shown in Table 1, the larger gpt-5.4 model generally achieves stronger performance than the smaller model across both domains, suggesting that stronger language mod-els better maintain goal consistency, profile consis-tency, and knowledge grounding during simulation. Our proposed realism metrics also show positive alignment with human judgments. For the gpt-5.4 

simulator, the hybrid setup generally achieves higher scores across most realism dimensions. The average realism score across three aspects has a positive Spearman correlation ( ρ = 0 .60 ) with the average human ratings in Table 2. Together, these findings suggest that our automatic realism metrics align with human perceptions of simulation quality. In the e-commerce example, the hybrid simulator responds to the product agent’s request for an order number by directly stating the number without un-necessary additional information. This triggers an error in the product agent, which concludes that the order cannot be found instead of asking an appro-priate follow-up question for account verification. A similar pattern emerges in the education do-main. The hybrid simulator uses API-based actions to retrieve more precise account and test-launch in-formation, allowing it to report an unexpected issue where the launch button is missing. This exposes a realistic limitation in the education agent, which falls back to generic troubleshooting steps rather than recognizing that the missing launch button may indicate a fulfillment or configuration problem. In contrast, the UI-only simulator repeats the prob-lems already suggested by the education agent, in this case, reporting a blank screen. Since the inter-action remains within the scope of the agent’s trou-bleshooting flow, the agent can provide a generic response and the failure mode remains unrevealed. 

6 Conclusion 

User simulation has emerged as a promising ap-proach for scalable evaluation of interactive agents, but existing frameworks are often limited to either UI-based or API-based interactions and provide limited methods for evaluating simulator quality. To address these limitations, we introduced VISTA ,a flexible evaluation toolkit that combines a hybrid user simulator with a suite of metrics for measur-ing realism, capability coverage, and interaction effectiveness. Through experiments in e-commerce shopping and online education environments, we demonstrated that the hybrid simulator produces more realistic and comprehensive evaluations than UI-only approaches while uncovering a broader range of agent failure modes. We further showed that our proposed metrics align closely with human judgments and provide meaningful signals regard-ing simulator quality and evaluation coverage. 87 Limitations 

Although VISTA improves the flexibility and com-prehensiveness of simulation-based agent evalua-tion, several limitations remain. Like other LLM-based simulators, our framework does not per-fectly model real human behavior. Recent studies have raised concerns about treating LLM-simulated users as human proxies. Zhou et al. (2026) show that LLM simulators can be overly cooperative and stylistically uniform, while Seshadri et al. (2026) find that simulated users may miscalibrate agent performance and surface different failure patterns than real humans. While our hybrid simulator in-creases interaction flexibility through combined UI and API actions, it may still exhibit behavioral bi-ases inherited from the underlying LLMs. Additionally, our evaluation is limited to two application domains: e-commerce shopping and online education support. Although these environ-ments represent common customer-facing agent settings, additional experiments across other do-mains such as healthcare or enterprise productivity would help further validate the generality of the framework. Besides, our proposed metrics primar-ily measure coverage and interaction quality rather than directly estimating real-world deployment suc-cess. Future work could investigate stronger corre-lations between simulator-based metrics and down-stream deployment outcomes. Finally, hybrid interaction introduces additional system complexity. Coordinating between UI-based and API-based actions requires routing logic and environment-specific integrations, which may increase implementation overhead compared to simpler simulation setups. Despite these limita-tions, we believe VISTA provides a useful step toward more flexible and comprehensive evalua-tion of modern interactive agents.

10 A Human Evaluation Details 

A.1 

To assess the realism of the generated user mes-sages, we recruited two graduate students as an-notators. Both annotators had prior experience de-veloping interactive agents, which qualified them to perform the task. For each use case, annotators evaluated user utterances from 100 conversations along three dimensions: human-likeness , coher-ence , and goal consistency . Each dimension was rated on a 3-point Likert scale. Inter-annotator agreement, measured by Cohen’s kappa, is 0.45 on average across the three dimensions, indicat-ing moderate agreement. The detailed annotation guidelines are provided below. Our data collection protocol was approved by the Institutional Review Board (IRB).   

> 1. Human-Likeness Definition : How closely the simulated user’s language resembles that of a human.
> Scale (1–3) :1 = Clearly artificial, awkward, or robotic phrasing. 2 = Somewhat human-like with occasional awkwardness or unnatural phrasing. 3 = Highly natural; indistinguishable from a real user.
> 2. Coherence Definition : How well the simulated user’s utterances follow logically from the conversation history.
> Scale (1–3) :1 = Often off-topic or logically inconsistent. 2 = Mostly coherent with occasional minor lapses. 3 = Fully coherent throughout.
> 3. Goal Consistency Definition :Whether the simulated user’s utterances remain consistent with the provided user goal.
> Scale (1–3) :1 = Frequently contradicts, ignores, or drifts away from the provided goal. 2 = Mostly follows the goal, but includes minor inconsistencies, omissions, or unnecessary deviations. 3 = Fully consistent with the provided goal across the conversation.

A.2 

Following prior work (Chopra et al., 2026), we conducted a blinded human evaluation on conver-sations from the education agent. Each conversa-tion involved the same education agent interacting with a user from one of three sources: a real cus-tomer, the UI-only simulator, or our hybrid simula-tor. Both simulators used gpt-5.4 as the backbone model. We recruited three graduate students as anno-tators. Annotators were asked to review only the user-side messages in each conversation and deter-mine whether they appeared to be written by a real human, generated by a bot, or too ambiguous to classify confidently. The evaluation included 225 conversations in total, with 75 conversations from each user source.
